# The Beach
### Adam, Present Day

As we step outside, my eyes dart across the landscape, and I begin to panic.

Water; so much water.

I had never seen anything like this before.

Ahead of me is the ocean. Turning around, I can see trees. I have seen trees in the Protectorate’s orchards, but nothing like these.

I collapse to my knees as I begin to regulate my breathing. Two in, pause for two, two out, pause for two. Looking around, I am relieved to see that my brothers are having a very similar experience.

“Try to slow your breathing,” Octavian says. “Steady, otherwise you might pass out.”

As my heart rate decreases, I begin to calm down, looking over the trees, beyond the forest, and onto the cliffs. They surround us on three sides, demarcating where the beach begins.

“Beautiful, isn’t it?” Sylvan gasps.

“Yes,” I reply, tears welling up in my eyes. “I never thought I’d see anything like this.”

As I wipe the tears away with my hands, my face droops as I remember our subdermal chips.

“What do we do about these?” I ask, pointing at my wrist.

“Removing them would be far too dangerous,” V says. “When the second Carrington event happened, the reason why the Protectorate was spared its effects was because of its shielding. That same shielding prevents wireless signals from making it in or out, and so, they are unable to track you on the outside.”

Gazing at V, I can’t shake the feeling that something is wrong. Is V just nervous, now that we have escaped? I know that I am. We had lived our entire lives as domesticated animals, and now that we are free, the realization began to scare us.

“The weather appears to be rather mild,” Octavian says. “It is likely spring, and so the elements will not immediately kill us; however, our priority is to find water and food, in that order.”

We adjust our backpacks and look around.

“Err, guys?” I say. “What is this doing here?”

To our left, I can see a Praetorian vehicle. It is a much larger model, similar to the one we escaped in the night before.

“I don’t think I’ve ever seen one this large,” says V, as he peers inside. “It looks like it has a weapons chest. Its contents may be incredibly valuable, even if the vehicle itself no longer works.”

“Can you get it open?” asks Sylvan.

“Maybe,” he says. Taking out a device from his toolbelt, he begins scanning it.

“It has a very faint energy signature,” he says. “It appears to be operational, but dormant. I don’t think it’s been used in years.”

He reaches out his hand and places it on the pad. The device turns red, indicating that his signature is rejected.

“It appears to be coded in a way that only allows some people, or maybe just one individual, to operate it,” continues V.

“Who?” asks Sylvan.

“I don’t know,” he replies, frustrated.

“Do you think you can still get inside of it?” I inquire.

“If I had all of my tools and days at my disposal, maybe. Given that we’re running away, I only brought basic tools. Not only that, but right now it’s more important that we don’t die due to thirst. We can always come back for it later,” V decides.

“Which direction should we go in?” I ask.

“There’s only one direction we can go in,” says V, pointing up to the distant forest above the hill.

“If we want to find water and food, a forest is a good place to start. Plus, we all have Atomizers, so wild animals won’t be an issue. Just make sure to set them to stun, so that we don’t accidentally vaporize our meal.”

Having done so, we begin climbing towards the forest.

“Quite scary, being out here finally, no?” V says.

Smiling, I know that V’s right. “Very much so, yes,” I reply.

We pass a faded sign that says Bonny Doon Beach. I wonder what that means.

I look over at Sylvan and say, “You’re being awfully quiet.”

Sylvan smiles and says, “I never thought I’d ever see the outside. It is rather surreal; I keep looking up and questioning whether the sky above us is real or just another synth-sky.”

I look up at the clear blue sky above, dotted with puffy white clouds.

“What’s that?” exclaims Sylvan. Underneath a bush, I can see a small creature, furry and white. Its nose twitches rapidly as it looks at us, its long ears shaking.

As it tenses its muscles, preparing to run away, the sound of an Atomizer rings out. The creature falls to the ground, stunned.

Octavian holsters his weapon as I approach the animal.

“Nice shot,” remarks Sylvan. “What is it, though?”

“According to pre-Carrington records, this appears to be a rabbit,” says V.

“Is it edible?” asks Sylvan.

“Very much so, yes,” V replies.

Octavian pulls a knife from his belt and slits the rabbit’s throat, warm red blood soaking its fur.

“Someone will need to start a fire,” says Octavian, as he begins preparing the animal. “Dry wood only, we want to keep the smoke to a bare minimum.”

I’m excited. I had never had real meat before, and to add on top of that, I had never seen a proper fire, either. In the confines of the Protectorate, open fires are a rare sight, and definitely not something you want to see.

Sylvan and I look around for dry wood, while V complains about his feet hurting. Not long after, we have a fire.

As the rabbit roasts, we look around, keeping an eye out for danger. What will the people above ground be like? Will they look different, after all this time? Can they even communicate? Has their language changed significantly to the point where we would have trouble understanding each other? Would they be violent? Perhaps welcoming? Maybe even scared of us?

“So, where do we go now?” I ask.

“We don’t yet know much about the above ground,” says Octavian. “We stick together, and we explore slowly.”

“Maybe we find people, maybe we don’t. We should make sure we don’t die from starvation in the meantime,” he continues, looking at the sizzling meat, now ready.

“This is spectacular,” I say as I taste the rabbit.

“This is real meat,” says Octavian, not the synth-meat you got downstairs.

“You’ve had real meat before?” I mumble, my mouth full.

Octavian looks guilty and says, “Yes. The S-ranks have real meat.”

“Huh,” I say, as I chew. I knew that they enjoyed special privileges, so I’m not particularly upset. Just one more drop in the ocean.

“So what makes you two S-ranks, if I might ask?”

“A C-class,” starts V, “possesses raw strength, which makes them very suited for manual labor. No offense, Sylvan.”

“None taken,” he replies, as he sinks his teeth into the rabbit meat.

“A B-class has intelligence, but no strength, which makes them suitable for management and bureaucratic positions. People such as yourself,” he says, as he points a greasy finger at me, “are born with both intelligence and strength, which makes you a capable law-enforcement class.”

“And then there’re the ones who make the rules,” I add.

“Precisely,” says V. “There are some among us who are born with exceptional abilities and impeccable genetics. Those are the ones that make up the S-class.”

“Good thing height is not one of the criteria,” smirks Sylvan. We all burst out laughing, except for V.

Resuming our hike, Octavian spots something through the trees. As we approach, the structure becomes clearer. It appears to be composed mainly of wood, with tall, elongated windows on either side. The glass is stained with depictions of old men with long beards, reading books.

“Perhaps this was a library?” theorizes Sylvan.

“Maybe,” says Octavian. “Whatever it is, we should exercise caution. It may have been a library centuries ago, but the building may serve a different purpose now. Check your Atomizers.”

We do so and approach the building slowly, listening out for any unwanted surprises.

As we step inside, we find a gruesome sight.
