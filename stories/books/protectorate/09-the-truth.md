# The Truth
### Adam

As we park outside of V’s home, my eyes widen to take in the sights. I had never seen a house like this.

“Welcome to my home,” he says, as the door slides open. The interior is even more gorgeous than the outside: hardwood floors, marble surfaces, and plush seats decorate the place.

Looking straight ahead, a glass wall reveals a large garden in the back.

“You have a garden?” I feel breathless.

“One of the privileges of being highborn,” he says, smirking. He opens the cryo-case and pulls out the vials of blood, emptying them into a machine.

“How do you have all of this?”

“Oh, do you really think A is the highest class?“ he asks in a mocking tone. He presses a few buttons on the device.

As the machine begins to spin, V performs a bow and says, “S-72, at your service.”

_S-class?_

I look at him in bewilderment. “There is an S-class?”

“Oh yes,” he says, “although there obviously aren’t that many of us.”

“How have I never seen your house before?” I ask.

“As an entry-level Praetorian, you do not have access to every area of Carrington,” Octavian interjects.

“The place is much larger than you know,” he says, pouring himself some tea.

We haven’t even begun decoding the data, and I’m already starting to realize just how little I know about my place of birth.

As Carrington is built in a system of caves, deep underground, it is difficult to know just how large it is. Yes, you can walk around, but certain sections are restricted unless you have business there.

I begin to realize just how much has been hidden away from me.

“Well,” V says, looking at the spinning device, “now we need to wait for a while. Once the data is converted into a digital format, I can begin reconstructing it.”

“Anyone want some ice cream?” he says as he walks over to the kitchen.

“Ice cream?” I ask.

“Oh, right,” he says, as he closes the freezer door, holding a large tub in his arms. “You’ve likely never had any.”

“Well, I had a mint the other day,” I say, unsure of myself.

V looks at me for a moment and then bursts out laughing.

“He had a mint,” he says, looking over at Octavian.

“Leave the kid alone. He’s still learning.”

Visibly annoyed, V opens the tub, revealing a soft, creamy substance.

“Grab yourself a bowl, kid,” he says, as he scoops some out.

I walk to a cupboard and take out a mint-green bowl before V drops a scoop into it.

“Erm, how do I eat this?” I ask, my cheeks flushing.

“Grab a teaspoon,” says Octavian.

I grab one from a drawer and dip it into the scoop, marvelling at its softness. I’ve never seen anything like this.

Picking up a small spoonful, I bring it up to my nose and sniff it. It smells wonderful, although I don’t recognise any of the ingredients.

“It’s vanilla, milk, and sugar,” says Octavian.

My eyes widen. I don’t know what vanilla is, but I’ve never had milk.

I bring it up to my lips and place the spoonful into my mouth. As my tongue swirls around, the coldness of the ice cream takes me by surprise. Once I get used to it, the sensation is wonderful.

The texture is soft and creamy, and the taste fills my body with joy.

“This is extraordinary,” I say.

“It’s not bad, is it?” asks V, smiling.

Is this what I’ve been missing out on? I thought I had it good as an A-class, with my own place, and occasional warm water for bathing, but this, this is something else.

“You are very fortunate,” I say to V, as I eat another spoonful of ice cream.

V smiles at me, although I can feel that he pities me, being so happy over what he must perceive as such a simple pleasure.

I don’t care. I’ve never had anything as nice as this before, and I intend to enjoy every single bite.

We sit down on V’s large couch in the main room, as the machine whirs away.

Looking around me, I feel guilty. We have yet to talk about the fact that my initial mission was to infiltrate them. I decide to breach the topic.

“So, during my meeting with the mayor, he told me something.”

“What?” asked Sylvan.

“He said that he intends to lower the age cap even further.”

Silence falls upon the room as I look over at Octavian. He looks surprised, so I don’t think he knew either. His graying hair indicates that he’s around the same age as the mayor, and, plus, this would not be a concern for him directly, but still, he did not know.

“He told you this?” Octavian asks.

“Yes,” I continue, “although I do not know why. He told me to keep it a secret, but, given the circumstances, I might as well share it with you.”

“We did suspect this was going to happen at some point,” says V, licking off the last bits of ice cream off his spoon.”

“However,” interjects Octavian, “we did not expect it to happen this fast.”

I shrug, not knowing what to say. It is interesting to know that the mayor kept this information from Octavian. Why would he tell me, though? Was it false information to feed to the rebels, in case I joined them? How would this false information benefit the mayor?

The machine stops spinning and plays a soft beep, indicating that it has finished. V jumps off the couch and sits down in front of his computer.

We don’t need an invitation, as we all get up off the couch and stand behind him. My hands shake as I await any new information.

“The material is still being sequenced,” he says, as he types on his keyboard, “but the computer has already managed to recover some footage.”

A video? I was expecting text, maybe the odd image, but video?

V hits a few more keys, and a window pops open on his screen; he presses play.

The video is silent, but the camera appears to float a few feet above the ground.

We stare at the footage in silence, as it shows a large body of water on the left.

“This looks like vehicle footage,” says Octavian, leaning in closer. “No sound?”

“No,” says V, “that part is corrupted.”

A hand appears in frame for a second. V immediately pauses the video, and we lean in, examining it.

V asks, “Is that—”

“The sleeve of a Praetorian Academy jacket,” I say, interrupting him. “It looks like he’s pointing to the mountain up ahead and to the right. When is this from?”

As my question rings out, the computer beeps.

“As luck might have it, I think we’ve managed to recover the date.”

Nobody knows what to say as we look at the year.

“Is—Is that correct?” Sylvan murmurs.

“Yes,” replies V, “I’m sure of it,” his eyes fixed on the number.

“2859,” whispers Octavian.

“Woo!” screams Sylvan, slapping V’s desk in excitement. “That’s only twenty-one years ago!”

I smile, as joy runs through me, like a river. If this footage is from only twenty years ago, unless something has gone terribly wrong, the outside is livable.

Sylvan wraps his arms around me and squeezes hard as we jump up and down.

Even Octavian, normally stoic and stone-faced, is smiling as he looks at the footage.

“Well, it looks like we have confirmation,” he says, leaning back into his chair.

“Who does that sleeve belong to, though?” I ask out loud.

“Who cares,” says Sylvan. “If he had the jacket on, that means he was likely just a little bit younger than you. Given that the video is from twenty years ago, that would mean that the owner of that jacket is long dead.”

That’s true. If he were still alive, that would make him around forty, and that would be ten years too old.

“Has any other data been recovered yet?” asks Octavian.

V leans back into his keyboard, typing frantically.

“Yes,” he says, as his eyes widen.

“What?” asks Octavian.

“It would seem,” says Octavian, “that the name of our town is inspired by a historical event.”

I move in closer, trying to make out the information on the screen.

“In what way?” I ask.

“Well, according to this document, a very long time ago, there was something called the Carrington event. A solar storm disrupted all technology present on Earth at the time, causing fires.”

“Is this how we ended up here, below ground?” I ask.

“I don’t think so,” says V. “According to this news article, a bunch of telegraph stations spontaneously caught fire.”

“What’s a telegraph?” I inquire.

“A very old type of technology, far too old to have been around when we went underground.”

“Did this event happen again? Could that be the reason?” I ask. “If such a solar storm happened once, could it happen a second time?”

I look down at V as he rubs his chin. “It is possible. The data is still being processed, so we can’t know for sure what it contains, not until it’s ready.”

“However,” continues V, “what we do know is that it is likely safe to go outside. We know that we have been lied to, and that whatever event caused our ancestors to seek refuge here, it has not made the surface unlivable.”

“You were right,” says Sylvan.

“Yes. Yes, I was,” replies V, pride evident in his voice.

As V’s gaze moves back to the screen, he suddenly shoots up straight, his eyes narrowing.

“What is it?” asks Octavian.

“This can’t be,” says V.

“What?” I find myself shouting.

“If these records are true, a few centuries ago, our population was much larger.”

“What?” I ask.

“Yes, look at these numbers. They are far higher than they are now. In fact, this graph looks rather worrying,” he continues, as his hands type frantically on his keyboard.

“You see, right here?” he asks, as he touches the screen with his finger. “This is a graph which shows the population of the Protectorate of Carrington.”

My eyes widen as I look at the data. “It was cut in half.”

“Yes,” nods V. “Something happened, which halved the population.”

“What?” asks Sylvan.

“I don’t know,” says V. “At least, not yet.”

As I stare at the graph, a thought occurs to me.

“V, can you find a chart of the average lifespan?”

“Why would I—” he stops in his tracks, his head whipping back towards the screen. He understood. He knows what I’m thinking.

“I think I’ve found something,” V says. “It’s not exactly what we’re looking for, but I understand what you want to know.”

V types in a sequence of characters, which pulls up a document. It looks old, very old, having been scanned in at some point.

The document is dated three hundred years in the past. My eyes shoot to the first paragraph, as I read it out loud, “Due to recent catastrophic events, leading to the loss of half of our population, a need has arisen. We can not currently maintain the level of output that we had before the event, and thus, the time has come to make a difficult decision. Even though our population has been halved, the infrastructure is still in place, and it will require as much maintenance as before, while older people still need to be cared for.”

I continue to read, “As a result, over the next few generations, an age cap will be instituted. It will come into effect slowly at first, as we cap the age of our citizens to seventy.”

Silence fills the room.

“Seventy,” I can hear V whisper.

Rage takes over as I walk away from the computer, screaming.

“Seventy!” I growl, my lungs hurting. “That’s what they capped it at first.”

My breathing now erratic, I engage in the Praetorian exercise, designed to calm me down. There is no use; everything has been tainted by them. The exercise, designed to calm me down, now angers me, knowing that it came from them.

Octavian puts his hands on my shoulders, telling me to breathe. “Take it easy, kid,” he says. “I get it. I do. Just take a deep breath.”

His words manage to soothe me somewhat, as I inhale and exhale, slowly, carefully.

“If we release this,” says V, pausing for a moment, “there will be chaos.”

Everyone looks at him as he continues, “I’m not saying that I agree with what they did, or with the fact that they kept this information from us, but I think we should take a step back.”

“I think you’re right,” says Octavian. “Let’s wait and see what other information we can gather before deciding how much of it we should make public.”

I want to fight, and scream, and tell them they’re wrong, that we should release all of it, immediately; however, as my heart rate begins to normalise, I know they might be right.

If I reacted like this, how would others react? What will happen if everyone finds out at the same time? How many people would die in the violence that would surely follow?

“Perhaps we shouldn’t upload it all at once,” I say.

“What do you mean?” asks Sylvan.

“Well, why don’t we drip-feed it? You first leak the data on the Carrington event, and then the video from outside, and then the age cap document.”

V appears to be lost in thought. “This might work,” he says, “but it will require some planning.”

“I disagree,” says Octavian.

Our heads turn towards him.

“We’re in a very precarious position. We’ve just exfiltrated data that the mayor would certainly like to get back. Also, a Praetorian died tonight.”

He tries not to look at me as he says those last few words, before continuing, “Given the circumstances, I say we leave—tonight. V, can you set up the machine to upload the data by itself, once it has been processed, even if it is incomplete?”

V nods at him.

“Right. In that case, I propose we do just that. Try to recover as much data as you can before we leave. You then schedule the data to upload, and by the time it is released, we should already be outside and no longer within the reach of the Protectorate. Does everyone agree?”

We all nod our heads in agreement. He’s right, we just don’t have the time. We don’t know what they have, and, since I am no longer a Praetorian, I don’t know how far along they are.

We have to assume they’re close to getting us, which means that we need to prepare to leave.

“I’ll get right on that,” says V, as he turns back to his computer. We all dissipate, leaving him to his work.
