# Gifting Day
### Adam

Morning light bathes the procession, lending their robes an eerie glow. At the head of the pack is the mayor, leading the men in our direction.

“How many do you think we’ll gain this time?” Sylvan asks as we watch their approach.

“We usually lose two and gain two, maybe three new ones back. We know we’re losing two today, but I don’t know how many we’ll gain.”

As the group grows nearer, I can hear them sing, their voices echoing through the valley.

_Cross, brave soldier,_

_Cross the mountains,_

_Cross the valleys,_

_Come, rest your weary head_

_For the road has been long_

_And you must be tired_

_Your responsibilities_

_Can now be shed._

“How far off are you?” Sylvan asks.

“Ten years. What about you?”

“Two,” he replies.

No further inquiry is needed. The number of years remaining in one’s life is a delicate subject, one that must not be explored with those who are nearing the end of their lifespan.

Their voices now louder, the group arrives at the orchard and stops. The man in front walks a few more steps, nearing the holes that we dug in the ground overnight, and turns to face the crowd.

“It is once more Gifting Day,” his voice booms over the orchard. “The Protectorate has charged me, the divine appointee and mayor of Carrington, with this most honorable of duties.”

He sighs and continues. “Centuries ago, our brothers above waged a terrible war; one which left the Earth desolate and inhospitable.”

“In its infinite wisdom,” he continues, “the Protectorate has built Carrington, this haven, underneath solid rock, enabling us to live our lives in peace.”

Listening very attentively, I wonder how much of the mayor’s speech I actually believe.

“However, for a society to flourish, we all must make sacrifices.”

The mayor takes a moment to look over the crowd before continuing, “Those who have gifts to offer, please step forward.”

Two men near the front of the group walk towards him.

“Come,” the mayor says. He indicates that they should stand near him.

“These brothers have reached the age of thirty, the moment in one’s life when he must offer the Protectorate the ultimate gift. This is the greatest of honours.”

The mayor’s ageing face and graying hair draw my eye as he delivers his speech.

“Due to limited space in our haven, sacrifices must be made.”

Has this always been the case? Were men always cut down at the age of thirty?

Reacting to the mayor’s words, four more men walk out of the crowd, each holding a baby.

“Behold,” the mayor says, “the reason for your sacrifice.”

The men look in awe at the four baby boys, happiness radiating from their faces.

“This is what you must give your life for.”

He walks over to each man, placing his hands on their shoulders as he looks them deep in the eyes. “Do you agree to this sacrifice?” he asks them.

“I do,” each one says.

Do you really?

Sylvan leans towards me and says, “Four for two this time. It looks like they’re ramping things up.”

The mayor reaches into his robe and produces a white tin containing two spherical capsules, a red plume swirling inside each.

The two men wrap themselves tightly in their white robes as the mayor places a capsule in each of their mouths. As they swallow, the rest of the crowd approaches and embraces them as their lifespan comes to an end.

Their bodies, now lifeless, are each placed standing up into the holes that Sylvan and I had prepared.

“As a final parting gift, these men will provide sustenance to the trees, the fruits of which we will enjoy for generations to come.”

Scanning the orchard, I count the trees that have produced apples. I am almost certain that year after year, fewer and fewer trees have given fruit.

Turning my attention back to the group, I bow with everyone else, honoring the two deceased brothers.

“Cherish your time,” the mayor says, “and use it wisely, for one day, you too will be called upon to make the ultimate sacrifice.”

The group lets out a guttural shout, bringing the ritual to an end, as the mayor glances at me before entering a vehicle and driving away.

I can’t help but wonder about my gifting day, just ten years from now.

“Sylvan,” I start, “what do you think about Gifting Day?”

“What do you mean?” Sylvan inquires.

“Do you think it’s right?”

Sylvan stops to think for a while. “Do we have a choice?”

“Obviously not, but that’s not what I ask. Is it right?”

My head turns towards the procession as they begin to drift away.

“It is unnatural, and everyone involved should be punished.”

My head whips back around to look at him. “What did you say?”

“I didn’t say anything,” replies Sylvan, “I am still considering your question.”

I’m not sure what to think. This is not the first time I have had this sort of auditory hallucination. The capsules that have been prescribed to me help, but I feel that these episodes may be occurring more often.

Sylvan’s voice was still ringing out as I turned my head to face him, even though his lips were not moving. I am almost certain of this.

Maybe I need a stronger dose.

“I suppose we must answer the call when our day comes,” says Sylvan, clearly resigned to his fate. “Wouldn’t you agree?”

“I do.”

I am fully aware of how hollow my words sound. This is easy to say when you have ten years left, but how must this come across to Sylvan, who only has two?

My thoughts are interrupted by a voice coming from my wristwatch.

“A-376, please respond.”

Lifting my hand to my face, I reply, “Yes, central?”

“A-376, the head Praetorian requests your presence at his office immediately.”

My heart skips a beat. Why would the head Praetorian want to see me?

“Looks like you might be in trouble,” says Sylvan with a hint of a smile.

“I hope not. Anyway, I think you have some holes to fill?” I look over at the graves.

“The work never ends,” says Sylvan, as he picks up the shovel.

“Until it does.”

Sylvan grins and nods his head in understanding as he walks away.
