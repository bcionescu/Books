# The High Ones
### Damian, Twenty-One Years Earlier

The two of us sit in the vehicle as it begins its drive to the Capitol.

To my right, Octavian leans towards the dashboard. His hand touches the windshield as he points out the peak of the mountain ahead.

“Have you ever seen anything like that before?” he asks, still breathless from earlier.

Given that this is our first excursion outside, something that neither of us knew was even a possibility until an hour ago, we need some time to get adjusted.

Feeling ashamed by the panic I experienced earlier, I decide to stay quiet and look out of the window to my left, at the inexplicably large body of water.

“I wonder how long the trip takes,” says Octavian.

“Roughly three hours,” replies Eve, making us jump.

“Hello?” I ask, perplexed.

“Hello, Damian,” replies Eve. “Shall we talk about the initial game plans for the negotiations?”

“What should we do initially?” Octavian asks.

“As the mayor informed you, I will act as your advisor. Throughout the process, I will drip-feed you more information, as knowing some things ahead of time will give the High Ones a perilous advantage.”

“Ok,” I reply, “so what is the initial game plan?”

“The first round of negotiation will include an accusation levied against them. We will blame them for releasing the virus that wiped out half of our population.”

“An accusation which they will surely deny,” says Octavian.

“Precisely,” replies Eve. “However, we assume they will give you a vaccine the moment you arrive. This will demonstrate that they are behind it, as they have an antidote for the virus.”

“They will surely claim otherwise,” I protest.

“It is unlikely that they will openly admit to releasing the virus and having a cure for it. They will attempt to frame the vaccine as a precaution against any diseases you may carry.”

“But since we’ve been underground, they’re the ones who may have diseases that can be dangerous to us,” replies Octavian.

“Precisely, and that is another way in which they may frame it. They could claim that the vaccine is intended to help you, not to protect themselves from a virus that they created and then unleashed upon the Protectorate. Either way, they’re covered.”

“So why the accusation, then?” I ask.

“The purpose of this accusation is not to get anything from them. They will never openly admit to genocide, even though they clearly possess the cure. Why else would they invite you into the heart of the Capitol? What the accusation will do is show our willingness to walk away. They have more to lose than we do, and so, they will likely call for a recess, but not actually end the negotiations. This will underline our upper hand.”

“An upper hand which they will never admit to either,” replies Octavian.

“Doubtful,” replies Eve.

“At the same time, we must make sure not to cross the line, as successful negotiations would bring advantages,” I add.

“Precisely,” says Eve.

“Is there any other information you can give us at this time?” I ask.

“No,” replies Eve. “Giving you any more data at this current stage will jeopardize the negotiations.”

I sigh, throwing my head back on the cushioned chair. “I feel trapped.”

“We’re certainly in a very perilous position,” says Octavian, looking out the window.

I feel tired; tired with the lack of choice, frustrated with the fact that my entire life seems to have been laid out ahead of me by others. I wonder how I would live, what I would do, if I had the freedom to choose. What would happen if I simply opened the door and rolled out, running into the wilderness?

I know I would never be able to approach people, as I would spread the virus that I have carried since birth. I can avoid people, take each day as it comes, drink water from streams, and find enough food to survive. It would be less comfortable than living in the Protectorate, but at least I would be free.

Both frightened and excited by the prospect, I wonder if the Imperatrix will be able to see these thoughts. Would she tell the Protectorate about them? Would she attempt to use them in the negotiation somehow?

As the vehicle approaches the Capitol, we find ourselves nearing the windscreen, trying to get a closer look.

The city is comprised of tall white towers. As we enter the city gates, we are immediately struck by the population.

“Can you believe how many people there are here?” asks Octavian.

“We’ve been inside the city for a few minutes, and I think I’ve already counted at least fifty people,” I respond.

“How can they live like this, with so many people around?” continues Octavian.

I do not know. The vehicle drives down an empty boulevard, wide enough to comfortably fit eight lanes, stretching all the way to the Palace.

Even though there are so many people, it would seem that most of them do not drive vehicles.

The entire city seems to be built around the Palace itself, as if all the other buildings are there merely to decorate the home of the Imperatrix.

Immediately visible from the gate to the city, this is a design decision that is not lost on us. The purpose of the wide road and the immense buildings is likely to give each person a sense of insignificance. No matter what you do, no matter how hard you try, you are too small, too insignificant, next to these large structures and wide open spaces.

As the vehicle reaches the Palace, we are stopped by the Guards. They inject us with an unknown substance, and then escort us to a private room with two beds.

“How long will we need to wait here?” asks Octavian.

The guard closes the door without a word.

“Well, the vaccine proves our theory at least,” continues Octavian.

“Do you reckon this is part of their negotiation strategy? Lock us up in here until we go mad, and then start the talks?” he adds.

“Likely,” I respond, pulling the device out and echoing the question.

“Yes,” replies Eve. “Uncertainty of one’s safety or freedom is a commonly used negotiation tactic. You will likely be held in this room for hours, or perhaps even days, as you await the start of the talks. Food may or may not be withheld.”

“We might as well make ourselves comfortable,” says Octavian, spreading himself out on one of the two beds.

I walk around, examining the room. It is very luxurious and comfortable; there is nothing to complain about in that regard, except for the armed guards outside.

“They ensured we can’t use our living conditions against them in the negotiations. This room is far more comfortable than anything we’ve seen in the Protectorate. If we complain about the guards outside, they will claim it’s for our safety,” I say.

“They seem to have it all figured out,” replies Octavian. “What do you reckon our chances of success are?”

I shrug and ask Eve.

“Approximately 7.04%,” she replies in a nonchalant tone.

Sitting on the edge of my bed, I realize that I have never felt one this soft. This is a far cry from my cot at the Academy.

“Do you think the other people in the Capitol enjoy similar standards?” I ask. I know I don’t have to clarify; Octavian knows exactly what I mean.

“If they do, maybe we can negotiate for resources. Maintaining a city this size must be material-intensive, and if the people are used to anything near this level of luxury, taking it away might be a surefire way to end up with unrest.”

“If that is the case, the current Imperatrix is likely not responsible for all this luxury. It is possible that her predecessors created it,” I reply.

“And now she’s stuck with the maintenance bill,” says Octavian, sitting up on his elbow.

“Precisely.”

With a gentle creak, the door opens as a gaggle of young women pours into the room, skipping and giggling in their white, flowery dresses. We sit up, uncertain of what is happening.

“Hello, boys,” says one of them, as she sits in Octavian’s lap.

“Yes?” I murmur, unsure of what to say, as a different woman plops herself on my lap, wrapping her arms around my neck.

“We think you’re bored,” says a third woman, as she wraps her arms around Octavian’s legs, resting her head on his lap, “and so we wanted to keep you company.”

“Do you mind terribly?” says a fourth, as she presses her chest into the side of my face.

We remain silent, aware of the trap, so we say nothing.

“Not very talkative today, are we?” says one of the women, pouting, as she brushes Octavian’s hair with her hand.

“Are you sad?” asks one of the women, her eyes wide, peering at me. “Shall we make you feel better?” Her hand slips into my shirt as she whispers in my ear.

I clear my throat and look at Octavian, who returns a similar look.

“Please leave,” the two of us say in tandem.

“What?” cries one of the women, biting the inside of her cheek. “But we just want to make you happy.”

“I’m sorry that you have been made to do this,” Octavian says, looking at her. The woman’s eyes widen in shock as she gets out of bed and stands up.

The women look at each other, unsure of what to do.

“You may leave,” I say. “You are all very charming, but you don’t have to do this. We’ll tell the Imperatrix you were splendid.”

The mood in the room suddenly changes. The women all get up in silence and walk out, making sure to put on the best smiles they can for the guards outside.

I feel demoralized.

My first encounter with women has been a complete tragedy. Enslaved women, much like me, were sent to wrap our minds in dense fog.

“We have our work cut out for us,” I say. We both laugh, punching our pillows in frustration.

“Do you think the room is bugged?” I continue.

“Likely,” replies Octavian. Looking around, we aren’t sure if the High Ones have the technology for it, but we might as well operate under the assumption that we are indeed being listened to; not that it would matter terribly, given the abilities of the royal bloodline.

“Eve,” asks Octavian, “something has just occurred to me.”

“Yes?” she asks.

“We know that the Imperatrix and Mater can see our thoughts. Will they openly admit to this?”

I look over at him in confusion.

“Please elaborate,” Eve replies.

“Well, we take it as a given that they know what we’re thinking; however, do they openly admit to being able to do so? Or do they never bring it up, or even admit to it, as this might indirectly give them more power?”

My eyes widen as I realize what Octavian means. We had assumed the royals would read our minds and openly call us out when we lie. But what if they don’t do that?

“This is an interesting question,” admits Eve. “Since we do not know how much of your thoughts they can make out, it is possible that they would indeed hide this ability from us. If they called out every thought, it would be trivial to figure out what they can and can’t read. However, if they don’t respond to your thoughts, or don’t even admit to being able to read them in the first place, they will have plausible deniability, and thus we will stay in the dark.”

“We can use this against them,” I say.

“What do you suggest?” asks Octavian.

* * *

### Damian

There is a knock on the door, followed by a guard.

“You are to join her Brightness, the Imperatrix, Daughter of the Sun, and the Holy Mater; may their rays never dim.”

Will we have to address them as such?

We jump out of bed and follow the guard to the meeting place.

“Where will we meet them?” asks Octavian.

“You will meet them in the Sun chamber,” the guard replies.

We follow him down a set of spiraling stairs, below the ground floor.

The temperature plummets with each step.

Shivering slightly, we enter the room. The guard indicates two small stone cubes, which are to be our seats, before retreating to a respectful distance. In front of our little cubes sits a large throne, currently empty.

The vast room, bathed in white noise, muffles our footsteps, which would otherwise echo off the empty walls. As I look up, I can see long, narrow slits in the ceiling, at the edges, where the sun shines weakly through. The geometry of the room causes the rays to reflect and bend.

The dappled sunlight dances on the walls, leading to the reflections off the dark river that runs through the chamber.

I walk towards the edge of the central platform that sits on top of the river itself. That’s what that sound is.

“Why do you think they call it the Sun chamber, if it’s so dark?” asks Octavian.

“The implication,” answers Eve, “may be that they control the Sun. Their religion is based on the idea that it could bring total annihilation once more. As long as they can convince people that they can control it, they stay in power.”

As the last word in Eve’s monologue left the device, we spot a dim light illuminating the corridor behind the door opposite to us.

As the light source approaches, the walls begin to shimmer brightly.

The Imperatrix walks through the entryway, bathing the room in soft, warm light. As she approaches, we are forced to lower our heads to avoid the blinding light.

“This is not good,” whispers Octavian. “We’ve just met her, and we’re already bowing our heads. I know that we have to resist it.”

Looking over at him, I realize we both have the same idea. We lift our heads in defiance, but close our eyes.

The light is rather pleasant, as I bathe in its warmth. Is that how the negotiations will go, the Imperatrix being the source of light and heat? Will this affect our perception of her?

Even though I am not able to see her expression at this moment, I know she is likely not pleased. Through closed eyelids, I can tell the light is diminishing, and so I dare to slowly open my eyes and lift my head.

In front of us sits the Imperatrix, having taken her place on the throne. Next to her, the Mater, her face obscured, wearing a finely-woven, black mesh-like fabric, a ceremonial veil, bathed in dim golden light that washes over her in waves.

Her time has come to an end, and so, she is not allowed to shine as bright as her child, the daughter of the Sun.

Our eyes now fully open, we look at the two women as silence fills the room. I do not know what to say, but we can not be the first to speak; that much I know.

“Are you pleased with our gift?” the Imperatrix finally asks. “I trust the dresses were to your taste?”

“The dresses, your Brightness?” I inquire. I’m not sure how we are to address the Imperatrix, but I think that this would be adequate, given her entrance.

A smile flashes across her lips as she continues, “The women I had sent over to your room, as you waited. Were they satisfactory?”

Can she view our memories, or only glimpse our current thoughts? If we answer, would she know precisely what had occurred? If we lie, would she know precisely how we are lying, or only that our answer is a lie?

“They were indeed very satisfactory,” replies Octavian, bowing his head slightly, in a way that indicated respect, but not obedience.

The Imperatrix does not seem pleased.

Can she tell that we’re lying, or is it Octavian’s partial bow that displeases her? My eyes shoot back at the Mater, who stands in silence. I hate the fact that I can not see where she is looking, her face obscured by the veil. Is she looking at me?

“I am pleased to hear that their performance has been adequate,” the Imperatrix says in a flat tone. “What are your names? Are you still not allowed to have them?”

The question takes us by surprise. Do we admit that we have taken names? Can she use this against us? If we give her our official Protectorate names, we would look weak. We peer at each other for a moment, knowing we have no choice.

“I am Octavian.”

“And I am Damian,” I reply. “And you?”

I notice that the Mater’s hand flinches briefly. Is she offended?

“You may refer to me as Imperatrix, or Daughter of the Sun.”

“Your Brightness, Daughter of the Sun, the Protectorate hereby accuses you of genocide,” I say, holding my breath.

If not for the sound of the rushing river underneath, the room would be completely silent. Did we go too far? Are we going to end up in that river?

“What is the meaning of this?“ the Imperatrix asks with a tremor in her voice.

She is attempting to remain calm. They’re desperate. Time to push a bit harder.

“Centuries ago, the High Ones released a virus into the protectorate, specifically targeting and killing all of our women. The Protectorate considers this to be a blatant act of sabotage,” I continue, desperately trying to keep my voice level.

“The High Ones have done no such thing,” the Imperatrix replies.

My eyes dart at the Mater, trying to penetrate the veil. What does she think of this? Is the slight tremor still present in her hand simply a sign of age, or a desire for savage violence?

“When we arrived,” I continue, “we were injected with something. This is clearly a vaccine against your biological weapon, as we would otherwise carry it.”

“You have indeed been vaccinated upon arrival; however, that was merely a precaution. Your people have been isolated for many years, and we can’t be too safe. The vaccine was both for your benefit, as well as ours, but we are not responsible for the event that killed off all of your women.”

“In fact,” the Imperatrix continues, “our intelligence suggests that there was no such cataclysm. We have reasons to believe that the Protectorate killed off its own women, and it is now attempting to blame it on us.”

Now this is something that I had not considered. Was the attack real? Until a few hours ago, I did not even know of the existence of women.

Regardless, the fact that she did not immediately have us killed would indicate that the High Ones want something from us, and they want it desperately. The question now is what?

They are unlikely to immediately come out and ask for it; of course, it will likely be one of the conditions they ask for towards the end, and they might try to frame it almost as an afterthought.
