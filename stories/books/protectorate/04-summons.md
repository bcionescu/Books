# Summons
### Adam

Adjusting my uniform, I near the door to the mayor’s office. Two large guards make way for me.

“Come in.”

The armored door slides up, revealing the inside of the Mayor’s office. A strange sound emanates from a device on his desk.

“Hello, boy,” says the mayor.

Slowly approaching, I bow, awaiting further instructions.

“My, what a well-educated young man,” the mayor says with a smile. “Please, sit down.”

He opens a metal tin and offers it to me as I sit in the chair. “Mint?”

Why is the mayor offering me a mint? It doesn’t even look like the synthetic kind, now long gone off the shelves, but like the real stuff, which I’ve only seen in archival films at the library.

“I see you’re cautious,” the mayor says. “Very smart. Still, indulge an old man and take one.”

As instructed, hands unsteady, I reach out and take a single mint from the tin, sliding it into my mouth. The refreshing feeling is exhilarating.

“What do you think? Good?”

“Thank you, sir. You honor me,” I smile.

Why am I being offered this? Is it a last gesture of goodwill before I am sent to my death?

The mayor smiles with satisfaction. His eyes fixate on me as he goes quiet.

Not daring to speak out of turn, I sit in the chair and wait, my eyes darting around the office, eventually settling onto the strange device in front of me.

The mayor follows my gaze and says, “Ah, the music. Do you like it?”

“I’m not sure what it is, sir.”

“It is an old tune from ancient times, called The Planets, by Gustav Holst. Do you know it?”

I can not help but be absorbed by the music. I have never heard anything like this before, but I know that the mayor is aware of that. Is he setting a trap for me?

Looking up, I can see his eyes studying my face, and suddenly, the sensation is back. Trying not to look panicked, I reach into my vest and take out the tin, sliding it open. Hoping the mayor can not see my hands shaking, I pinch a capsule and swallow it along with the mint.

The mayor watches with interest, but says nothing.

“Sir,” I say, aware of my heavy breathing, “Music is, of course, banned, and so, I have never heard any before.”

The mayor smiles as his fingers caress the small music player. “Are you going to have me arrested, A-376?”

Suddenly reminded of my role as a Praetorian, I straighten myself and lower my head slightly. “No, sir, of course not.”

“Would this not be against the rules? I’m fairly certain you have to arrest me, now that you have found me in possession of contraband.”

I think it over. The mayor is playing with me, but why? Did I do something wrong? Why is the most powerful man in the Protectorate toying with me?

“While it is against the rules to have contraband, I would imagine that, as the mayor, you enjoy certain privileges.”

The mayor smiles and leans back. “I like you; you’re rather bright.”

Is he toying with me still?

“Thank you, sir,” I find myself blurting out.

“A-376. Quite an interesting name, isn’t it?”

“It’s the only one I have, sir.”

“Why is that your name?” he asks, fingers laced loosely over his abdomen.

I look up at him, uncertainty clouding my expression.

“Sir? I do not understand.”

“Why do we have such names? Why not Alexander, or Theodore? Why A-376?”

My mind is racing. I feel that I am in peril, but I have no notion as to how I can escape. The mayor clearly wants an answer, so I have to be careful.

“At birth, our brothers are placed in classes based on their genetic profile. Those with physical strength only are placed in category C, those with brains, but not brawn, the manager class, are placed in category B, and those of us who have both are placed in category A.”

Looking at the mayor’s physique, I suddenly realize that he does not look like any other bureaucrat I have ever met. As a former Praetorian, he has both an impressive physique and a keen mind.

Taking a deep breath, I continue, “Our category, as we belong to it, is the first part of our name. The number following it is our identification number. I was born the three hundred and seventy-sixth member of the A class, hence my name.”

“Very good. You quoted the manual almost perfectly. However, my question remains unanswered. Why do we have this system?”

I had wondered this throughout my life; however, I have to be cautious. It is evident that the mayor is looking for a specific answer, and I am not sure which one it is.

“The system is put in place as a punishment, sir. Our ancestors destroyed the world above with atomics, rendering it uninhabitable. Through the wisdom of the Protectorate, some escaped underground, creating our current way of life.”

“Very good,” the mayor says. “You are correct that the world above us is destroyed. However, I notice you’re wearing an Atomizer. How do you reconcile those two facts?”

Looking down at my weapon, it occurs to me that I had never considered this. If atomics did indeed destroy the world above, leaving only the Protectorate below, why do we use weapons and vehicles powered by atomics?

Lowering my head, I answer, “I’m afraid I do not know, sir. The founders of the Protectorate are far wiser than I, so I defer to their judgment.”

A shadow of a smile appears on the mayor’s lips. “Tell me, have you secretly chosen a name for yourself?”

I am in danger.

I had, of course, but can not admit to doing so. The tin in my pocket burns as I think of the inscription I have etched into it.

“No, sir,” I murmur. I know I sound unconvincing.

“You know, it is rather common for members of the A class, such as ourselves, to take on unofficial names,” the mayor says. “I, for one, have taken the name Damian; the head Praetorian, your superior, is Octavian.”

Damian looks at me with a smile, expecting something. After a brief pause, he continues, “Tell me, if you were not alive at the time of the wars, why do you have to pay for it? Should the sins of the fathers not remain with them?”

Yet another trap. Beads of sweat begin to appear on my forehead as I search my mind. Why would he ask this? What is he after?

“I think I know the answer, sir, but I am not certain.”

A grin appears on Damian’s face as he leans towards me. “Well? Tell me.”

“I believe the system plays a dual purpose. It is both a punishment and a way to ensure no other brother wars happen.”

“Go on,” Damian urges me.

“The founders believed that the wars were not started over scarcity of resources, but because of individuality. Instead of working together, humans chose to pursue self-interest, eventually leading to near extinction.”

After pausing to catch my breath, I continue, “I suspect the naming system and the way our society is structured as a whole is designed to strip us of our individuality, assigning us to a group, and disallowing any signs of personality, such as names, choice of clothing, or taste in music.”

As I give the last example, I freeze. Did I go too far? Damn it, I should not have mentioned the music. As the mayor leans back in his chair, I remain seated, expecting his punishment to come at any moment.

The music from the small device in front of me intensifies, as Damian begins to applaud and shout “Bravo! Bravo!”.

Feeling confused, I look up at the mayor.

“You truly are a bright man,” Damian says, grinning from ear to ear. “That is precisely it, and that is the reason I have called you here today.”

“Sir?”

“As you have astutely pointed out, the reason for the system is to prevent individuality from spreading once more, ensuring harmony and prosperity for all of our brothers in the Protectorate.”

As Damian leans back in, placing his hands on his desk, his face changes. I squeeze the arms of my chair.

“However,” he continues, “there are those amongst us who seek to destroy that hard-won balance.”

“Rebels, sir?”

“Yes, very good. Do you know any?” he smirks.

“Oh, no, sir. I do not.”

“Well, that’s too bad, but worry not. You will soon know all of them.”

“Sir?”

My knuckles turn white.

“You’re a smart lad. You’ve shown me that you know the true purpose of the system. The rebels are smart as well.”

My heart sinks. Am I about to be executed under suspicion of being a rebel?

“Sir?” My voice trembles.

“As I have pointed out, the rebels are a smart bunch; misguided, but very intelligent. However, their numbers are few, and so, they’re always on the lookout for new members.”

Damian gets out of his chair and starts wandering around his office.

“How old are you, boy?”

“Twenty, sir.”

“Ah, what a fine age to be. How old do you think I am?”

I’m not sure. Not many people as old as the mayor walk about, so it is difficult to tell.

“I would assume you are in your forties, sir?”

“Hmm, precisely right,” the mayor says, standing in front of a second door which I did not notice when I came in. “Precisely right.”

I wonder where that door leads.

“I am forty-one,” he continues, “however, the age of the average citizen is capped at thirty. So how can this be?”

“Even though our lives are capped to prevent overcrowding, those who have proven themselves useful to the Protectorate are granted an extension.”

“Those who have proven themselves useful,” the mayor echoes, lost in thought.

“You are, of course, right. I have proven myself useful to the Protectorate, and thus, I have been granted indefinite leave to life. Hopefully, I’ll die a natural death, many years from now.”

“I am very happy for you, sir.”

Damian continues his walk around his office. “If you’re twenty now, that means that your life will end roughly ten years from now. Have you considered how you will spend this time?”

I’m not sure what to say. Nothing about my life had ever involved any choice. Those were all made for me, including when everything would end.

“I will aim to do my best to serve, sir, ensuring that humanity continues to exist long after my death.”

Damian wanders over to his desk and sits on the edge, looking down at me.

“Our lives involve very little choice, but sometimes, an opportunity shows itself. This is what happened to me, long ago, before you were created. I saw an opportunity and took it, granting me long life. What would you do if you were given a similar opportunity?”

I do not know what to say. Is the mayor honest? Is he truly about to give me an opportunity at long life, or is this yet another game by this clearly very twisted man?

“I would jump at the opportunity to serve the Protectorate, sir, whether that involved a reward or not.”

“Good,” Damian says, as he sits back down in his chair.

“I have such an opportunity for you.”

“I am honored, sir, but why me?”

“As you pointed out, our system is based on control. To know how to exert it best, I have found that data can prove to be invaluable.”

Turning to his side, he types a few words on his keyboard.

“We keep files on every citizen, including you, A-376. Would you like to see yours?” he asks, grinning.

I had heard of such files existing, but I have never been able to confirm it.

“I’m not sure if that is my place, sir.”

“Oh, don’t be modest. Here, I’ll show you,” he says, as he turns the display around. Gazing at it, I can see my name, as well as my age (20/30), next to a photo of me from the Academy.”

“Scary, isn’t it,” Damian says, “to see your age next to the number at which it is capped.”

I swallow, but say nothing as I continue to read.

Citizen A-376 has shown remarkable physical and intellectual capacity, in line with his extraordinary genetics. Upon finishing his schooling at the Praetorian Academy, he was appointed to a starter law enforcement position.

However, due to his extraordinary mind and constitution, it is recommended that he be given type one missions.

I wonder who wrote that.

“See that,” Damian says. “That’s quite the recommendation letter.”

“Thank you, sir. I am honored.”

Damian looks at me for a moment and then says, “Tell me, how do we come into existence?”

“I’m not quite sure what you are asking, sir.”

“How were you created? How did you come to be?”

The question confuses me. “Like all humans before me, I was grown in an incubator.”

“Correct,” Damian exclaims. “All of us were grown in incubators; however, the process is not perfect. There is variance between brothers, of course, which accounts for the different classes. We are the product of a lottery of sorts, determined the moment our first cells come into existence.”

He hesitates for a moment and then continues, “What if I told you that rebels doubt this?”

“Doubt what, sir?”

“Some of them claim that this is not true, that we were not always grown in incubators.”

“I’m confused. How else would we come to be?”

“Once again, you are correct. The logic is simple. How can we come to be, if not through the only possible way? Now, the rebels are highly intelligent; however, if you wish to fool someone, it is easier to fool a smart person, as they will, at the very least, consider your proposition.”

“What can I possibly do about this?”

“Ah, yes, yes, we finally arrive at the reason you are here. Your task is simple; the Protectorate wishes for you to infiltrate the rebels, by any means necessary, and obtain as much information as you can.”

Damian sips a drink of water and continues, “You are to find out who they are, what information they think they have, and you are to report this back to me.”

I am unsure how to respond. On one hand, this is an honor. Completing this mission would ensure that I will live a hopefully long, natural life. However, I have no clue how I can find these rebels.

“Sir, as I’ve stated earlier, I do not know any of them. I’m not even sure where to start.”

Damian smiles as he reaches for a folder on his desk. Opening it, he continues, “Lucky for you, we have some info already. The rebels have attempted to bribe some of your colleagues, who have dutifully notified us. We believe they plan to steal explosives from a construction warehouse and blow their way to the outside.”

“Would that not kill us all? The radiation has yet to subside.”

“Indeed, it would,” Damian says. “This is why this mission is so critical, and why, in turn, you would receive an indefinite life extension. So, what do you think?”

For a few moments, I sit and ponder. “I think that the explosives heist is a good way for me to infiltrate them.”

“Elaborate,” Damian asks.

His mouth is curved in a smile.

“A heist in a Protectorate facility would require a large team, but not on the ground. Most members would be working from afar, swiping and copying security cards, disabling cameras remotely, that sort of thing. For the actual heist, only one or two people might show up.”

“Go on.”

“When they do, I can arrest them, or at least pretend like I intend to. As I will take them by surprise, they will not have a chance to draw any weapons they might have. Because of that, they will try to plead their case. I will indicate my intelligence by asking questions, instead of shooting them or calling for backup. This will give them hope of escape, and it will allow me to earn their trust by letting them go.”

Looking down, I continue, “If they can gain access to such a facility, they likely have a hacker within their ranks. It will not be hard for them to track me down after the fact. I believe that once I let them go, they will try to recruit me.”

“Splendid,” Damian says, “well done. We agree. It’s good to see that the profile is correct about your intelligence.”

“Thank you, sir.”

“That settles it, then. We will proceed as planned. Any crimes that you commit during your time with the rebels will, of course, be pardoned, and if you are successful in uprooting the rebellion, you will get to live a long, happy life, dying of natural death one day far from now.”

“Thank you, sir,” I smile.

“Once you have obtained the information, I will expect a full briefing. The heist is planned for tomorrow night. You are excused from your normal Praetorian duties until further notice. Go home, get some sleep, and good luck, boy!”

“Thank you very much,” I say, as I stand up and bow. Turning towards the door, Damian’s voice sounds one final time, “Oh, one more thing.”

I turn to face him.

“You may have noticed that in this morning’s Gifting, we sacrificed two and replaced them with four.”

Although I have noticed this, I say nothing.

“There has been an increase in child production, and this will put a strain on our population. As a result, we will look to lower the cap from thirty years to twenty-eight. Your ten remaining years are looking shorter, I’m afraid.”

Trying not to betray my surprise and anger, I freeze in place.

My voice shaking, I ask, “Why not lower production?”

“That, I am afraid, is simply not possible. If I were you, I’d make sure to get this mission right, so that you don’t have to worry about the new cap.”

He smiles at me and adds, “Oh, and let’s just keep this between you and me.”

Stepping outside the office, I keep walking until I can move no more.
