# Answers
### Adam

I can hear the Mayor stand up and hesitate for a few moments before making his way to the front of the vehicle.

He sits down in the seat next to me.

“How is she doing?” I ask.

“Not good,” the Mayor replies. “She needs urgent medical care.”

“Which we can’t give her at the Protectorate,” I add.

“No,” he replies.

GG chirps sadly as he rolls around the vehicle.

“Why not?” asks Ambrosia.

“Because the citizens of the Protectorate are unaware of the existence of women. Taking her there would raise far too many questions,” I reply.

The Mayor nods in agreement.

“So what do we do now?” Ambrosia asks.

“The medical pod can keep her alive for now, although she is quite badly injured. I am not certain of how long she has,” replies the Mayor.

“So she’s going to die?” Ambrosia asks, tearing up. “She can’t, she helped us escape.”

“You should seek help at the Convent,” the Mayor replies. “They will be able to save her there.”

“The what?”

“The Convent,” begins Ambrosia, “is where the Maters retire to, after they have served their term.”

“How do you know of this place, and that we should go there?” I ask.

“Your mother told me,” the Mayor replies.

I look over at him, unsure of what he means.

“My mother?”

“I’m sure that by now,” the Mayor adds, as he glances at Ambrosia, “you may have figured out that humans did not always use Incubators. Everyone used to be born of a mother and a father.”

“Yes, I know that we come from the Source. My mother is likely stuck in a Life Tank somewhere, producing yet another child.”

The Mayor’s face contorts in a sad smile as he looks at me.

“You’re half right. Your mother is currently in a pod, but not in a Source. In fact, she’s only a few feet away from you.”

I stare out into the distance, unsure of how to reply. I can feel Ambrosia turn around to look at the pod.

“And you know this how?” I ask, my voice trembling.

“A year before you were born, Octavian and I were sent on a mission to engage in negotiations with the High Ones. We were successful, or at least, it appeared that we were at the time. That’s how I received my life extension, as well as Octavian.”

“This still doesn’t answer my question,” I say. “How do you know she is my mother?”

“Well,” the Mayor hesitates, “it turns out that the Mater, then the freshly crowned Imperatrix, found herself blushing in my presence. She hasn’t aged a day by the way.”

“And?”

“She was due to be artificially inseminated, but she did not wish for that to happen. She expressed her desire for choice, for freedom, and admitted that she would like to have that child with me.”

I can understand her desire for freedom.

“You are the father of the current Imperatrix?” Ambrosia asks, her eyes widening.

“No, not quite. The current Imperatrix is actually not Katherine’s first child.”

“Who was?” I ask.

“You were,” the Mayor replies.

I finally muster the courage to look at him. The Mayor, my father, looks back at me with kind eyes.

“The High Ones could not allow for a son,” Ambrosia whispers.

“Exactly. Their ability to peer into one’s mind is inherited exclusively on the female branch of the family, hence why they have only had women on the throne. You were the first male child to be born in their family in centuries.”

“And so she could not keep me,” I whisper.

“Precisely. When you were born, your mother messaged me privately and notified me that she would send you down to the Protectorate.”

“So you’ve known this entire time?”

“Yes, I have.”

“Why didn’t you say anything?”

“Would you have believed me? How would I begin to explain the situation to you, without first telling you what women are, and what has happened above ground?”

“This is why you sent me on the mission, to infiltrate the rebels?”

“Partially, yes. When I showed you your file, I did not display the entire document. The system flagged you as a potential rebel before you graduated from the Academy, isn’t that right, Eve?”

“That is correct, sir,” rings a voice from the control panel.

“Eve here recommended your expulsion and termination. That was very naughty of you, Eve.”

“I apologize, sir.”

“Before your profile could be reviewed, I intervened and removed the data. However, that nightmare revealed an opportunity. Your psychological profile showed that you would naturally sympathize with the rebels, and thus, you were the perfect candidate for the mission.”

So it is Eve who writes the profiles on every student. I wonder how far her influence goes.

“The perfect candidate?” I ask.

“Yes. I knew you’d sympathize with them. Both your mother and I have a deep desire for choice and freedom, and I suspected that this trait would be transferred over to you as well.”

“So when you caught wind of the rebels, you sent me in, hoping I’d escape with them.”

“Precisely,” says the Mayor.

“This is why you let us go,” I say.

“Correct,” says the Mayor.

“Why did you show up by yourself, at the very end, when you let us out? Why risk it?”

“Well, I had two reasons. One, my word carries a lot of weight. If I made it there first, I could tell the Praetorians that you’d escaped and that you lay dead outside. This would mean that they would not investigate further.”

“And the second?”

“I wanted to see my son one last time,” he replies.

Unsure of what to say, I turn my head back towards the road, as we sit in silence.

“What were those capsules about?” I ask. “The ones I had to take.”

“Oh, Eve helped me out with those,” the Mayor replies.

“I sure did,” Eve adds.

“Those capsules,” continues the Mayor, “were intended to suppress your gift of sight.”

“My… gift of sight?”

“Yes. When your mother messaged me, informing me that she would send you down to us, she said that you had inherited the gift of sight, that she had been communicating with you throughout her pregnancy.”

This would explain the strange incidents I had encountered, hearing people’s thoughts.

I turn around and look at my mother, lying peacefully in the pod, and the realization hits me. I had seen this before, in one of my dreams. I had seen a woman, drenched in blood, appearing to be dead. Just like before, I had lacked the context, but I have seen it.

“Is the sight limited to peering into one’s thoughts?” I ask.

“What do you mean?” the Mayor asks.

“Does it extend past that? Does it allow you to see things before they happen?”

“Have you experienced this?” the Mayor asks, his eyes wide.

“I’m not sure. Could it be possible?”

“The powers of the High Ones grow over time, with each generation. I suppose it is theoretically possible for you to have inherited an evolved form of the gift.”

“And I assume the pills were meant to stop me from having symptoms, as that might have led to me being studied, or even dissected.”

“Yes,” admits my father.

“You got your gifts from your mother, and your looks from me,” he adds, with a rogueish smile. “We’re getting close to the Protectorate.”

As the vehicle comes to a stop, I recognise the beach where we first made our way out.

“You’re not coming with us?” I ask.

“I’m afraid not. The Protectorate still needs me, and besides, if it were discovered that I broke you out, many innocent people would have to pay the price.”

I understand. I am not the only person that he is responsible for.

“According to your mother, the relationship between the High Ones and the Convent has been growing tense, although she did not explain why. This might be a good thing. By now, they likely know what has happened, and they’ll know you’re coming.”

“What should we expect?” I ask.

“I’m afraid I do not know. Eve,” he continues, as he turns his head towards the control panel. “Can you confirm that you have the coordinates?”

“Yes, I do.”

“Fantastic. Well, I guess this is goodbye,” my father says, as he shakes my hand. “I hope I’ll see you again, boy.”

The next thing I know, he sits up and exits the vehicle.

“Eve, take care of them, ok? That’s an order.”

“Yes, sir.”

The door shuts, and the vehicle starts on its way to its new destination.

“What do you think we’re going to find there?” Ambrosia asks as she moves up front with me.

“I don’t know. However, what I do know is that my mother will die if she does not receive help soon.”

As the warm early morning light sneaks into the vehicle, I look back over my shoulder. She looks strangely beautiful in the medical pod, drenched in her own blood; pale, suspended in red.
