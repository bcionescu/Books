# Choices
### Imperatrix

“What are your thoughts on the negotiations, mother, now that they have concluded? Did we act in our best interest?” I ask.

The Mater speaks, her veil moving in rhythm with her mouth, “I believe this is the best possible outcome, given the circumstances. The power of our enemies grows each day. The Protectorate still poses a threat, but one we can extinguish, given the newly negotiated Pact.”

She takes a breath, continuing, “When their population collapsed, much of their infrastructure had to be shut down. This meant that anyone who could not contribute would become a burden. Thus, they introduced the age cap, lowering it over time.”

“And so, at any given moment, we can simply send them more children, forcing them to make difficult decisions,” I add.

“Yes. They hoped that we would increase their workforce, as young people would be able to support the infrastructure and, by extension, an older population. However, all we have to do is provide them with more children over time, more than they can handle. If they refuse them, we can cut them off, leaving them in a weaker position.”

“Would they not find themselves in the same position they are now?” I ask.

“Incubators introduce genetic defects over generations. If they did not already experience more defects, they would not have agreed to the terms.”

“So it makes more sense for them to simply take the children and lower the age cap further, rather than be cut off completely, and die out in a few generations.”

“Precisely,” replies my Mater. “Besides, we will squeeze them from both directions, sending them more children, and making them dependent on our genetically modified seeds, which will produce smaller yields over time.”

“Thus, they will be forced to cap the life span of their citizens even further, weakening the Protectorate. They walked away thinking they won the negotiation, but in a single generation, the Protectorate will be far weaker as a result.”

My mother nods.

“Tell me, mother, what did you think of the two envoys the Protectorate sent?”

My Mater’s eyes dart in my direction. With each generation, our powers grow stronger. This is a blessing, of course, but at the same time, it made it more difficult for the Maters to read the thoughts of their daughters. Peering into my mind, I know she can sense something is amiss, but the fog must be too thick.

“I think you should put them out of your mind. You will never see either of them again. Besides, it is time for us to select a donor, a suitable male whose genetics will produce the next generation.”

“What happens if I do not produce a daughter?” I ask.

“Then the pregnancy must be terminated, and you will begin anew. Our gift has only ever awakened in the female side of the bloodline. There would be no place for a boy, and thus you must try again, until a girl is produced,” my Mater says.

I sigh. “I knew you would say something to that effect.”

My Mater’s voice softens as she continues, “My dear Katherine, you shouldn’t worry. Once we have selected a suitable candidate for you, and a girl begins to form, you will fall in love with her before she is even born. A psychic bond will form while she is still in the womb, and you will be terribly happy.”

I am lost in thought. Throughout her life, I had never had any choice, any say. My life was laid out for me. When I reached the age of twenty, my mother stepped down as empress and became a Mater instead, as I, her daughter, became Imperatrix. One day, my daughter will become empress, and I will be relegated to the role of a Mater, before eventually being sent out to the Convent.

I desperately find myself wishing for a choice in the matter, or, at least, one more choice. Unbeknownst to my dear Mater, I have already exercised my freedom by sleeping with one of the Protectorate envoys.

If I go through the donor selection process sooner rather than later, I will be able to mask the fact that I am with child, as I have masked my first missed bleeding.

What if it is a boy? What then? If that is to be, I must follow my Mater’s wishes and terminate the pregnancy. In that, I do not have any choice.

### Imperatrix

“Your Majesty,” the man bows as I enter the cold, sterile room.

I know I look nervous. How can I not be? A donor had been assigned to me, and now, I am to be artificially inseminated. In a minute, this very kind man—with likely very warm hands—will ask me to lie down, as he impregnates me with an unknown man’s genetics.

How clinical and disgusting.

“You will be pleased to hear,” he says, “that we have found a suitable candidate for you. He is a perfect genetic match who will bring many desirable qualities to your daughter.”

“That is wonderful,” I say, my voice cold and flat.

“Everything is ready. Do you wish to proceed?”

Do I have a choice? Why did he ask that question? He knows I do not have any choice in the matter; I can sense it. I can feel his anxiety as he wonders whether I’m peering into his thoughts right now.

My brow furrows as I take a deep breath. Does he have any choice?

“I am as ready as I will ever be,” I say.

“It is perfectly normal to feel some anxiety before the procedure. I assure you, it is perfectly painless.”

He walks over to a metal table and continues, “If you are ready, you may lie down, placing your feet on these supports.”

I climb on the table and put my feet in place, lifting my dress.

The geneticist sits down in a swivel chair and places himself between my legs as he rubs his hands together.

At least I was right about the warm hands.

“You will feel a small pressure,” he says, “as I insert something soft. As I mentioned before, there is no need to worry, as this will not hurt.”

I inhale sharply as something thin and somewhat cold enters my body. I turn my mind away from his, not wanting to know the specifics of what is happening.

Instead, I think of the man from the Protectorate; handsome and dangerously intelligent.

“I will now deliver the genetic material,” he says, as a cold fluid is slowly injected into my uterus.

Peering into the geneticist’s mind, I can see the face of the man whose genetic material is being inserted into me. He is handsome, I can see that, but he pales in comparison to the man who will be the father of my child.

“We’re done,” says the geneticist, as he slides out the device and hides it from sight. I stand up, unsure of where to look.

“May you have a healthy daughter,” he says, as he bows.

“Thank you,” I say, jumping off the table and patting down my dress.

Walking over to the door, I open it and walk out, escorted by my guards.

Did I do the right thing? I am already pregnant, so I know this procedure will have no effect. The geneticist did not suspect a thing, or if he did, he somehow hid it from me.

Was he able to tell that I am already pregnant? If so, would he tell Mater, or would he keep quiet, on account of me being Imperatrix now?

He did not comment on the fact that I am not a virgin. What does it matter to him?

Maybe there is a reason for how the laws work. You are born, and you observe your mother and grandmother. When you reach twenty years of age, you begin your term as Imperatrix for twenty years, and then as an advisor for twenty, transferring your power to your daughter.

I doubt that the Prophet would have foreseen this circumstance, an Imperatrix becoming pregnant with someone from the Protectorate, but still. The way in which he wrote the laws certainly made it easier for me to get away with this.

Who knows what someone dead for centuries actually thought? I decide to put this out of my mind. I will have a daughter, and everything will be well.
