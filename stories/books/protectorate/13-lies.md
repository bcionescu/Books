# Lies
### Octavian

We burst out laughing.

“This is no laughing matter,” the mayor says, “as you will soon find out.”

Our laughter comes to a stop. I look over at Damian, who seems as incredulous as I am.

“Yes,” the mayor says, “you will meet with them. This is why you have been summoned. You are to go above ground and enter negotiations with them.”

Hesitating, Damian opens his mouth to speak. “Why us? Sir, you would certainly be far better equipped to handle these negotiations.”

“Sure,” the mayor says, “but I also know far more secrets than you two. So far, I’ve only told you what the High Ones already know.”

“The High Ones?” I interject.

“That is what they call themselves,” replies the mayor.

“Of course,” says Damian. “If you met with them, they could extract anything from your mind.”

“Precisely,” says the mayor, “which is why I can’t go. To tell you the truth, I envy you boys. I hear she’s very pretty.”

“She?”

“The Imperatrix. Every twenty years, like clockwork, a new empress is crowned. Once she ages out, her daughter becomes the new empress, and she becomes an advisor.”

“How do they know that the daughter will have the ability to read minds as well?” asks Damian.

“The ability manifests itself only on the female side of the bloodline. A male donor is selected at the appropriate time, usually when they turn twenty, and each queen has one child, a girl, who will eventually become her successor.

“How can they ensure it’s always a girl?” I inquire.

“That, we’re not quite sure of. Even though we have the upper hand technologically, they have regained some lost technology as well, including making some further advances into genetics,” the mayor says.

“Is this also how they obtained their abilities?” asks Damian.

“Smart,” the mayor says with a smile. “It is possible that they increased their powers over time, but they had to have some to begin with, to seize power. How much they had, and how much they have been able to acquire, is unknown.”

“Their genetic technology,” continues the mayor, “is likely what allowed them to wipe out all the women in our colony.”

“Why would they do that?” asks Damian.

“To weaken us,” says the mayor. “If we can no longer reproduce, we have to rely on the incubators.”

“Why can’t we make women?” I ask, having just thought of it. “If the incubators can make men, why can’t they make women?”

“The virus never went away,” the mayor says. “We are all carriers. We tried making them after the attack, but they all died soon after birth; more victims of the virus.”

“That is also why we can never leave the colony, not in large numbers,” says Damian. “If we did, we would unintentionally commit genocide, killing the women above, and thus ending humanity for good this time.”

The mayor nods in approval.

“What are we to negotiate with them?” I ask. “Should we ask for women, or a cure?”

The mayor shook his head. “Why would they give us women, when it is they who took them away in the first place? Of course, they’ll never admit to it, but you should know this.”

“What should we ask for, then?” says Damian.

“Men, or more specifically, boys,” replies the mayor.

“Huh?” I mouth.

“We need to up our numbers,” says the mayor. “If we can’t have women, we’ll take more men. This will put us in a position of dependence on them, of course, which is what they want, but what choice do we have?”

“I assume we would offer them technology in return,” says Damian.

“Precisely; however, you are only authorised to offer them certain types. It would be foolish to equip them with our best technology, as they already vastly outnumber us,” replies the mayor.

“What is to prevent them from killing us off completely? Can they not theoretically genetically engineer the boys they send with a new virus, wiping us out?” asks Damian.

“Very clever, but no. If they had wanted to wipe us out, they would have done it with the initial virus. They need us because they want our technology, which would be useless without our scientists.

“So we are to offer them inferior technology; in exchange, they will increase our population.”

“Precisely,” says the mayor. “If we get more people, we will have more manpower, and so we can start increasing the cap on the lifespan once more. It’s clear they want something from us; otherwise, they would not have kept us alive, and they would not have invited us to these negotiations. In the past, we have failed to reach an agreement, but this time, we think we have a chance.”

“Why?” asks Damian.

“All the previous negotiations took place before the virus outbreak. Why invite us to negotiate now? They need something, and they need it bad.”

“This would be a perfect opportunity to find out if it is actually they who released the virus,” says Damian.

“How so?” asks the mayor.

“If they’re guilty, the first thing they’ll do is vaccinate us against it.”
