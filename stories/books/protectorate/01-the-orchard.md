# The Orchard

My pupils mirror the synth-sky as I look up. Clear and moonless, small shimmering white sparks dot its fluid form.

Breathing in, I can smell the rancid aroma of apple trees permeating the air. The undertones of the scent make me sick, knowing its origin. Unpleasant tingling begins to manifest along my spine, towards the base of my neck, and I immediately remind myself that I ought not to think of such things.

The tingling immediately subsides.

I advance throughout the orchard, my path illuminated by the light of my glow-globe, hovering inches above the ground. The globe spins around, attempting to perform a backflip, and lets out a concerned digitally generated chirp.

“I know GG. I will be fine.”

It is quiet out tonight. With each step, my Protectorate-issued boots clang against the cobblestone path, the echoes of which reverberate dissonantly against the tall stone walls of the cave—a tomb in which the Atomic wars above ensured everyone I have ever known would reside.

Once more, I look up at the synth-sky. How can that sky exist? We are deep underground. As I contemplate the nature of the technological hallucination above, the tingling returns.

Breathe.

This time it is too late. The tingling sensation that has crawled up through my spine and towards the base of my neck now feels like fire.

Beginning to hyperventilate, I collapse to the ground, hands clenched around my head, blinded by the light of my glow-globe, now dashing around in zigzags, worriedly chirping.

The pain is unbearable. The searing sensation expands into my brain, as every synapse screams desperately. The sensation is made only worse by the ear-splitting sound, emanating from my mind, reaching outward. A cacophony of frequencies, each competing for which could inflict more suffering.

With my hands shaking, I reach into an inside pocket of my uniform jacket and take out a small red tin. On the front, I can see the symbol of the organization that has kept us safe for generations, depicted by a hawk, with its wings outstretched. In one claw, the bird holds a scepter; in the other, a sword.

As the symbol shines in the light, my hands, covered in sweat and dirt, manage to slide the tin open. I pinch one of the milky white sphere-shaped capsules and swallow it.

Immediately upon ingestion, my body begins to relax. The fire has almost instantaneously subsided, and my mind has gone quiet. All that is left now is the cold breeze, soothing my sweat-covered face, and GG, circling me excitedly, his chirp manically oscillating.

Thank the Protectorate and their medicine, they tell me, without the aid of which these episodes would eventually consume me.

Turning the tin around, I can see the name Adam—my name—etched into the back. This is a dangerous thing to do, but it is something I must do nonetheless. If one knows that he is not allowed a name, like an animal in a factory farm, inevitably, he finds one for himself.

A rustle near me draws my attention, making me sit up, light reflecting off the black and red of the Atomizer in my hand. GG swivels around, a hidden compartment sliding open, to reveal a small electric shock weapon.

“Beep beep,” says GG.

“Who’s there?” I shout. “Identify yourself, citizen.”

“C-5445,” says the voice. The man approaches with his hands up in the air.

“Citizen, you are breaking curfew.”

“I have been tasked with preparations for Gifting Day.”

“Very well.” I lower my weapon.

The man relaxes and lowers his hands.

“Are you alright?”

“I am, and I would appreciate it if you kept anything you saw to yourself,” I growl.

The man looks down at my hand. Following his gaze, I realize that I am still holding the tin, with the name Adam etched onto it, visible in GG’s light.

I curse, knowing I’ve been careless. This man not only witnessed me in a vulnerable state, but he has also seen my name. Maybe he will not make the connection? Perhaps he’ll think it was my number, and not a name?

“Is that your preferred name?”

I do not know what to say. I am in trouble either way. A Praetorian should never appear weak in front of the citizenry, and he should definitely not have a name other than the one assigned to him by the Protectorate. Both are grounds for severe punishment if reported.

“I’m Sylvan.” The man stretches out his hand towards me.

What should I do? If I meet his hand and shake it, I would acknowledge that I do have a preferred name. At the same time, this man is taking a severe risk, as the consequences for a class C taking on a name are far worse.

I shake his hand, replying, “Adam, born A-376.”

“Nice to meet you, Adam,” Sylvan smiles. “Now you know I won’t speak of this.”

That is true. This man is now at a higher risk than I, so I can count on him to keep his lips sealed. The question is, why did he do that? Why did he reveal his preferred name? Is he insane, or perhaps he has an ulterior motive?

Regardless, I decide that I like him. Very few people would take a chance like that, and even fewer people would choose not to report you, forgoing a generous reward in the process.

Sylvan walks over to a utility chest and places his hand on the palm scanner. “Well, the work won’t do itself.”

The container opens with a soft hiss, allowing him to take out a shovel.

“Would you like a hand?” I find myself asking.

“Are you sure?”

The man knows that, as a Praetorian, I am an A-class. Manual labor is reserved for the C-class, and therefore, this is not part of my responsibilities.

“Yes.”

I walk over to the container and pick up a second shovel. “It’s a quiet night, and I think the exercise will do me good.”

“Well, if you want to help, I won’t stop you,” Sylvan says with a smile.

“How many are we preparing for Gifting Day?”

“We need two holes,” Sylvan replies.

“Then let’s get started.”
