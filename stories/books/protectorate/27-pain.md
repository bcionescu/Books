# Pain
### Adam, Present Day

I am awakened by the sound of a door slamming against the wall, as heavy footsteps approach.

Here we go again.

I breathe heavily as I stand up on my cot.

The guards stop outside of my cell and tap a code into the panel, lowering the force field. One of them makes a sign with his Atomizer, indicating that I should come out.

How did he get an Atomizer? Is that one of ours?

I step outside of the cell and allow one of the guards to cuff me, as the other raises the field back up. Looking down, I notice that both of the guards have the same type of thin golden ring around their index fingers.

My vision becomes suddenly blurry. One of the guards has hit me in the head, screaming, “Eyes forward.”

As my vision adjusts, I notice the large creature sitting on the floor. Its eyes are red, demented, giving me the impression that it will rip me to shreds the moment it is commanded to. I had encountered a similar one when I was arrested.

Examining its features, I am struck by how human it looks. It has two arms and two legs, although they are altered significantly. Its eyes, although devoid of any thought, are also clearly human.

What did they do to this person?

Seconds later, I find myself back in the same padded room that I was in the day before, strapped to a chair.

I can see the word Grunwald stitched onto that man’s uniform. Is that his name?

“Well, well,” he says, showing me a nearly toothless smile. “I see you’re back for a visit.”

He cracks his knuckles and punches me squarely in the nose, blood gushing in every direction.

“Sorry about that,” Grunwald says, “I didn’t give that one my all. We all have to warm up, you know? You’re my first body today.”

Body.

“Now, a little birdie told me yesterday that you lied to me. In fact, two little birdies did so.”

Grunwald implies that both V and Sylvan talked. V? Yes, of course. Sylvan? No. I know Sylvan did not tell him anything.

“I know you’re lying,” I say. My lips are covered in warm blood.

“Huh?” says the torturer, walking over to me and grabbing hold of my hair. “You calling me a liar?”

Same gold ring on his finger.

“I know Sylvan didn’t tell you anything,” I reply, staring defiantly at Grunwald.

The man’s menacing look turns into a smile as he lets go and walks around the chair, opening a drawer.

“Yeah, I suppose there’s no reason not to admit it. Sylvan was not very forthcoming. V, on the other hand, was the chirpiest of birds.”

“Was?” I ask.

“Yeah. He couldn’t take quite as much as you two,” Grunwald says, as he picked up a syringe and a small vial.

Walking back in front of me, he opens the vial and begins extracting a clear liquid from it.

“You see,” he continues, “he had a seizure, and died, the poor little birdie. He chirped no more after that.”

Grunwald taps the syringe, removing the air bubbles, and walks over to me. I want to know what is in the vial, but I will not give him the satisfaction of asking.

Whatever it is, I have no control over it, so I let it go. I will soon find out, anyway.

The man sticks the needle in my arm and empties the syringe.

The room starts to become blurry, Grunwald’s voice distorting as he walks around the room.

“Yesterday we tried the fun methods of questioning—fun for me, that is, hehe. Since you did not want to talk, now we do it the other way.”

What more can I do?

“What more can you do?” asks Grundwald. “Oh, little birdie, you see, you’re about to chirp for me.”

What’s happening?

“What is happening,” says Grunwald, “is that your mind now belongs to me. While you are under the influence of this truth serum, you will be unable to have private thoughts of any kind. Every idea, no matter how small, you will vocalize.”

No.

“Oh, yes. Very much so,” Grunwald grins. “Now, do you want to know a secret? Why don’t we immediately use this on people?”

Damn you.

“Well, that’s not very nice,” says the man, as he punches me once more, causing pain to shoot through my body.

“You see,” continues Grunwald, as if nothing had happened, “the serum has a high chance of killing you.”

How high?

The man grins, “About 70%.”

My heart sinks.

I only have a thirty percent chance of survival?

“Now,” continues Grunwald, “there’s nothing that says I can’t administer the serum twice. What do you reckon the odds of surviving twice would be?”

The man smiles and leans towards my face, “Very, very small.”

“So,” he continues, as he walks away, “you’re now going to tell me everything I need to know. If I am not satisfied with your answers, provided you live, I will administer another dose. If, by some miracle, you survive that one, I will administer a third, and so on.”

“You see, the Pact forces us to hand you back to the Protectorate if you are found. However, who’s to say that we found you at all?”

The Pact?

Grunwald’s eyes narrow, hurrying up to me, wrapping his hand around my throat. “Don’t you play games with me. You know what the Pact is.”

I look at him in desperation, my eyes widening, unable to breathe.

“Huh,” says Grunwald, releasing his grip. “You actually don’t know what it is. Interesting, it looks like they’ve suppressed this information.”

What is this man talking about? What is the Pact?

“Maybe this will jog your memory,” Grunwald says, as he walks circles around me. “About twenty years ago, two young representatives of the Protectorate came here and negotiated a deal with us. They were to give us technology, and we were to give them people, and genetically engineered seeds.”

My eyes widen.

That’s why the Life Tanks look like medical pods; they’re based on the same technology. But people?

“Yes,” says Grunwald, “people. Where do you think you came from?”

I am unsure what to believe.

What about the incubators? Isn’t that where we came from? Did I actually come from a Host, from here, above ground?

“You do have incubators,” says Grunwald, “but they are terribly inefficient, often producing non-viable babies.”

The image of the disfigured brother in the medical pod, used to store data, flashes through my mind.

“As a result, two of your people, Damian and Octavian, negotiated the Pact.”

My eyes widen.

“Oh, I see those names ring a bell,” he says, smiling. “Who are they to you?”

I can’t help but picture Damian, the Mayor, letting us go, and Octavian coming with us, but disappearing before we were ambushed.

“I see, interesting,” says Grunwald. “That Damian fellow became the Mayor.” He grinned, leaning away from me.

“Do you know why he let you go?” he says, barely able to contain his laughter.

Why?

“Because, according to the Pact, if we come across you, we have to send you back.” Grunwald laughs an unhinged laugh as he slaps his large belly.

That’s why he let us go. He knew we would be returned immediately.

“Precisely,” says Grunwald, still laughing, as he wipes away a tear.

So I am as good as dead. No matter what I do, I either get killed here or I get killed by the Protectorate.

On top of that, I will talk; I have no choice.

“That about sums it up,” says the man, “although I’m not sure if we are going to return you or not.”

Why?

“Well, you may have gotten lucky. Your other friend, Sylvan? He’s already received this second dose,” the man says, grinning at me.

“The serum doesn’t take you out quickly, you see, and right now, he’s in his cell, probably dying. It was worth it, though; we got a whole lot of information from him. The serum’s quite nice, isn’t it?”

I look at him, my mind blank. I do not want to share anything else with this man.

“Because of that,” he continues, “we’ll hold off on the second dose with you, assuming you make it through the first one, of course, hehe. If you all die, we’ll admit no fault and say we never found you. If either of you lives, we’ll pack you up and send you back home. We’re nice like that.”

So I am dead, one way or another.
