# Beginnings
### 723 years earlier

I look down at my shoes, as my heart pounds in my chest. After all these years, I may have finally found an investor, someone who believes in my vision.

Why did he invite me here? What are his motives? Why is he so interested, after many other investors called my idea a dead end?

I may not have a working prototype yet, but the science is solid. I just need a chance.

“You may now go inside,” a calm disembodied voice says.

As I lift my head, the door swooshes open, warm light spilling into the dimly lit waiting room.

“Please, come in,” a woman’s voice rings out.

I stand up, my legs shaking, and I step into the large room, bathed in golden light. My eyes immediately dart to the man, sitting behind the desk. His eyes are fixated on me, but he does not invite me to sit down.

“Please, take a seat,” the woman next to him says.

I look at her, and then at him, unsure of what to do. Who do I speak to?

The woman smiles and continues, “My employer is not too fond of speaking, I’m afraid. Although he is present, you will be speaking to me exclusively for the entirety of our meeting.”

“Oh, I understand,” I say with an unsteady voice.

The woman indicates a chair across from them, and I sit down, shifting my gaze to her.

“Thank you very much for coming, Mr. Eriksson. How do you find the Institute so far?”

“Thank you for inviting me,” I reply. “I find it to be rather surreal. I have heard rumors of such a place, but I was not certain of its existence.”

“Oh, it very much does exist,” replies the woman.

“I understand you are interested in my technology?”

“Yes, we are. We believe it can do many good things.”

“Many good things?” I ask.

“I apologize, was my phrasing unclear?” she asks.

“No, it’s just that you used the word good, not great, or profitable, but—good.”

The woman smiles, her eyes shining with joy. “I see what you mean. Yes, I did indeed mean good. You see, Mr. Eriksson, my employer is not concerned with such pursuits as profit. He has… other interests.”

“Such as?” I ask.

“My employer believes that the human species finds itself at a precipice, a dangerous point within its existence. We believe that your technology could serve as a form of backup, so to speak, in the event of a cataclysmic event.”

My eyes shoot over to the man, who has not taken his eyes off me, before I focus my attention back on the woman.

“So you would like to use my technology as a way to ensure that if civilization is almost destroyed, all human knowledge is retained.”

“Precisely,” the woman responds.

“I suppose my invention could have that side effect, yes. However, there is a problem. As you may be aware, not only do I lack funding, but there is a crucial part of my technology that I have yet to figure out, a piece without which the technology would be impossible.”

“You mean the ability to accurately record and reproduce a human’s thoughts and feelings, for their consciousness to be transferred to Eden?”

“Yes, precisely,” I answer.

As a strange voice echoes inside my head, I can feel my heart skip. My eyes dart to the man sitting in front of me.

_Worry not, Mr. Eriksson. We are here to help._

**End of Book One**
