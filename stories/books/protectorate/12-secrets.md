# Secrets
### Damian, Twenty-One Years Earlier

I adjust my collar in the mirror of our shared dormitory. As a fresh graduate of the Praetorian Academy, I am looking forward to receiving my own apartment. It will be small to begin with, but at least I won’t have to share anymore.

I turn around to see Octavian lying down in his bed, hands under his head, looking at the ceiling.

“Damian, why do you think we’ve been summoned?” Octavian asks.

“We’re the top two graduates,” I answer, sliding my hands in my trouser pockets. “However, it is strange that we’d be summoned like this by the mayor.”

I lay down in my bed and look out the window at the synth-sky. “It might be a mission?” I ponder.

Octavian chuckles. “Unlikely, we’re as green as can be.”

“It’s out of our hands,” I say. “We’ll have to wait and see.”

As if summoned by my words, a younger colleague knocks on our dormitory door.

“Come in,” I answer.

The young boy opens the door slightly and says, “There is a vehicle outside waiting for you.”

Octavian and I look at each other with raised eyebrows. “Thanks,” says Octavian. We both jump out of our beds and walk out the door.

“Huh, they sent a vehicle for us,” continues Octavian.

“Never been in one before,” I admit.

“Neither have I,” Octavian chimes. “All the other boys are going to hate us,” he adds, with a grin.

I smile and nod.

Walking down the stairs of the academy, the doors of the vehicle open as we approach, and we climb inside. The driverless vehicle closes its doors and begins sliding along the empty roads that lead to the mayor’s office.

“Do you think we’re in trouble?” asks Octavian.

“I don’t think boys who are in trouble get to ride in vehicles,” I reply.

“No, not unless they’re in _really_ big trouble,” says Octavian. We look at each other and burst out laughing.

Arriving at its destination, the vehicle comes to a stop and soundlessly opens its doors, allowing us to jump out.

A Praetorian is waiting for us. As we approach, he indicates that we should follow him as we walk into the building.

On our way to the mayor’s office, I rapidly become aware of the differences between this structure and the rest of Carrington. The floors are dark cherry wood, and the walls are marble and glass.

“Is this real wood?” I ask.

The Praetorian indicates that I should be quiet as we enter the elevator. I immediately close my mouth, although my eyes continue to dart around the splendour of the space I now find myself in.

Looking over at Octavian, I fail to see the same wonder in his eyes. Is he just not interested in the luxuries that power affords you? Why does the rest of Carrington not look like this?

“Through there,” the Praetorian says, showing us an armored door. “Knock first.”

We approach the entrance, adjust our uniforms, and knock. The door slides up, revealing the mayor at his desk.

He looks up at us and says, “Come in and sit down.”

We walk in, each taking a chair.

He looks at us over his desk, his sallow eyes darting left and right.

“Do you know why you’re here?” he asks.

We look at each other, and then back at the mayor, shaking our heads.

“You must have _some_ notion,” he insists.

I clear my throat. “We suppose it may have to do with the fact that we’re the top two graduates of the Academy?”

A smile flashes over the tired mayor’s face. “Go on,” he says.

“We think it may be some kind of mission,” adds Octavian.

“You are both correct,” says the mayor.

“We are wondering,” Octavian continues, in a hesitant tone, “why _we_ would be summoned, given how green we are.”

“The reason why you have been called is because of how green you are.”

“I don’t understand,” I murmur.

“The enemy you will face,” says the mayor, “is unlike any human you have encountered.”

“Enemy?” echoes Octavian.

The mayor’s eyes wander to him, and he asks, “What if I told you a secret so hidden that uttering it in public would get you instantly atomized?”

We look at each other, but say nothing.

“What if this secret shook the foundation of everything you believe and know about the Protectorate?” he inquires, his eyes darting over to me.

We remain silent, unsure of what to say.

“Sir?” asks Octavian.

“Remind me,” says the mayor, as he leans back and throws his feet on the desk, “what is it that we teach in history class nowadays?”

I hesitate before opening my mouth. “The former states above us, on the surface, destroyed each other in an atomic war, rendering the Earth uninhabitable. In its great wisdom, the Protectorate built this shelter, shielding its best and brightest, so that one day we may return outside and repopulate.”

“Repopulate,” echoes the mayor, smiling.

“How are we supposed to do that, exactly?” he asks.

“The way humans always have,” says Octavian, “with the incubators.”

The mayor nods, echoing his last word. “Incubators.”

“And how did we make more of us before the incubators?”

“Before?” I ask.

“Our ancestors, the ones who got themselves blown up above ground, were so advanced that they could travel between planets. However, that was not always so. Technology gradually advanced over a long period of time, meaning that at some point, they did not have the incubators.”

The mayor pauses, breathing in deeply, and continues, “So, how did we make more humans before the incubators were invented?”

We sat in silence, noticing that we’ve been doing that a lot since stepping into the mayor’s office. It has just occurred to me that I had never considered this. Looking over at Octavian, I see the same expression on his face.

It makes sense that humans could not have always been technologically advanced.

The mayor looks at his computer and says, “Eve?”

“Yes, mayor,” answers a voice as sweet as honey.

“How are you today?” he asks.

“Quite fine, sir. My circuits are in perfect working order.”

The mayor smiles, looking up at us. “What do you make of this?”

Our eyes, now wide in shock, look over to the machine. I have never heard a voice such as that; one unlike any that our brothers possess.

“The truth,” continues the mayor, “is that men were not always the only humans. Centuries ago, this bunker was populated by both men and women,” he says, emphasising the last word.

“Women?” we ask in concert.

“Eve,” the mayor started, “can you bring up images of women, please?”

“Certainly,” she says, whirring away. “Done!”

The mayor turns his screen around as we lean in. On the display, I can see brothers unlike any I had witnessed before. They have long hair, large smiles, and lumpy chests.

My stomach catches fire as my eyes wander.

“Lovely, aren’t they?” the mayor asks rhetorically, with a sad smile.

“What happened to them?” asks Octavian with anger in his voice.

“They were all killed,” the mayor says, turning the screen back around to face him.

“How? When?” I cry out.

“Centuries ago,” says the mayor.

“What killed them?” interjects Octavian.

“A virus,” replies the mayor.

“How can a virus target women specifically?” I ask.

“Naturally, that is unlikely to happen. However, the enemy is very skilled in such technology.”

The realization that viruses can be weaponized caused us to lean back in thought. How is this possible? Why have we not been told about this?

Octavian straightens himself and asks, “The enemy?”

The mayor sighs, cracking his neck, and says, “Yes, the ones living above ground.”

Our eyes widen in surprise.

“But,” I hesitate, “there is no one above ground.”

“That is incorrect,” says the mayor. “Humans never stopped living on the surface.”

I do not know what to say, as frustration wells up in my chest.

“So the nuclear fallout was survivable?” asks Octavian.

“There was never any nuclear fallout. Well, there was some, but that was incidental. It was not due to a nuclear war.”

The mayor goes silent, looking at us and smiling. “I must admit, sharing this with someone feels better than I thought it would.”

Octavian looks visibly confused, just as I am, and we become angry.

“Why have we been lied to?” I cry out, squeezing the arm of my chair.

“Pipe down, young man,” says the mayor. “I didn’t say there wasn’t a disaster, I just said it was not atomic in nature.”

I lean back into my chair, relaxing slightly. “Then what happened?”

“A millennium ago,” begins the mayor, “in 1859, the Carrington event took place.”

“Like our city?” interrupts Octavian.

The mayor smiles. “Yes, the city founders sure had a good sense of humour. Indeed, the city is named after this event.”

“Now,” continues the mayor, “in 1859, the largest solar storm ever recorded took place. Solar flares have an interesting property, which is that they interfere with technology. At the time, this was concentrated in telegraph stations—a rudimentary way to communicate.”

“What effect did it have?” I ask.

“It made technology catch fire,” says the mayor.

“All the telegraph stations caught fire?” asks Octavian.

“Not all, but some. This was a major annoyance, but in 1859, it posed no real danger. The fires were put out, the stations were rebuilt, and that was the end of it.”

“And how did this cause a disaster?” I inquire.

“The disaster,” continues the mayor, “did not happen for a few more centuries. Eventually, this event was due to repeat itself. However, when it happened next—”

“Technology was far more widespread,” interrupts Octavian.

The mayor nods. “Indeed. The next solar storm had the same effect on technology, causing electronic devices to catch fire; however, this time, the Earth was covered in tech. It was in our homes, government buildings, infrastructure, and satellites; everywhere. When the storm hit, it destroyed all of it. Even though only a small percentage of it caught fire, that was enough to set civilization back by millennia.”

“Then how come _we_ have technology?” I ask.

“Because the Protectorate built this facility with that eventuality in mind. The large bunker in which we have spent our entire lives is shielded, thus all technology in it survived. Everyone was moved in after the event took place, right as the violence began.

“So that part is true,” says Octavian.

“Yes,” nods the mayor. “A disaster did occur, just not the one that you are told about.”

“Why lie about it, though? Why keep us here? Why not go up after the violence had died down?”

“The violence did not die down for generations. Additionally, many diseases that had been almost wiped out returned, killing even more people. The founders of the Protectorate thought it best to give it some time.”

“Give it some time?” growled Damian. “How long has it been?”

“Approximately five hundred years,” says the mayor in a bored voice, “give or take a few decades.”

Silence envelops the room.

“You may wonder,” the mayor continues, “what has stopped us from going back up since.”

We nod, unable to speak.

“As the power went out, the infrastructure collapsed. Humans had relied on automated farming and complex delivery systems to ensure they had food. Once none of it worked, people began killing each other. Next, the packs of dogs began feeding on the corpses, and then, the people that remained.”

“Packs of what?” asks Octavian.

“Dogs. They were a popular type of pet that humans kept, domesticated over thousands of years. Once people started dying, the dogs began to revert to their natural state, forming packs as they became hungry. Most people died off, either killed by other humans or by the dogs. If they escaped both, disease was likely to take them at some point.”

“But, I assume, things eventually stabilized?” I inquire.

“Eventually, yes.”

“So then why can’t we go back up?”

“Things stabilized,” continued the mayor, “because of one family; one which now rules the above.”

“How so?” asks Octavian.

“This level of chaos,” says the mayor, “was unprecedented. Yes, civilizations had collapsed before, but never like this. Not on a world scale. We believe that the trauma awakened something in the genetics of this one family, an advantage which allowed them to rule over everyone else.”

“What was it?” I ask with bated breath.

“The capacity to peer into one’s thoughts.”
