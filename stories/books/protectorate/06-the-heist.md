# The Heist
_Adam_

The sound of a Lumina drone passing by my window wakes me. Did I fall asleep? Is there still time before the mission? I check my watch and realize I will be cutting it close.

Once I got back from the bar, I lay on my bed and fell asleep for much of the day. Thankfully, this helped clear the alcohol out of my system. My mind feels clear and sharp, which is a good thing, provided I want to survive.

I take off my civilian clothes and throw on my Praetorian uniform. Grabbing the Atomizer next to my bed, I step out of my apartment and head for the facility where my future will be decided.

Outside my building, a vehicle is waiting for me. As I approach, it beeps pleasantly, and the door opens, allowing me to slip inside. GG greets me with a digital purr.

“Hey there,” I smile. “I missed you.”

GG lets out a few high-pitched squeaks.

“Off we go.”

I input the destination into the navigator. The door closes, and the vehicle begins to slide noiselessly down the street.

“So, GG, what are my chances of success?”

GG spins and lets out a chirp.

“That low, huh?”

As the vehicle finds its way through the city of Carrington, I look out the window at the people scurrying back to their homes. While the vehicle isn’t camouflaged, it might as well be, as nobody seems to pay it any attention. I wonder why that is. Are they simply disinterested? Are they conditioned not to inquire, or are they perhaps afraid to look, scared of drawing the attention of a Praetorian?

Likely a combination of all three.

The vehicle beeps. My eyes shoot down to the navigation panel. The dot that represents the vehicle is moving over a map of the city, nearing the facility.

Unholstering my Atomizer, I make sure it is set to stun. If the rebels take me by surprise or do something foolish, I want to make sure that I can take them in alive.

The vehicle comes to a stop outside the facility, and I turn to GG. “Listen, you’re going to have to stay in the car, ok?”

GG chirps a sad digital tone, taking out its small shock weapon.

“I know, you want to come with, but you’ll only get in the way on this one, I’m afraid. I should go it alone.”

With the press of a button on the control panel, the door swings open, and I step onto the empty street, now past curfew.

Atomizer in hand, I watch the vehicle drive away, GG furiously floating around inside of it.

Sorry, buddy.

I check my wristwatch. Seven twenty-three. I’m not sure when the rebels will attempt to infiltrate the facility, but I know it will be past curfew.

Approaching the building, I wait to be scanned.

Nothing happens.

The rebels have taken the building offline, disabling the security system.

Using my hands, I manage to pry the door open and slide inside before closing it back up. If I have to run, I’ll find that to be very difficult. Once inside, I receive confirmation that the facility has gone dark. There is no power, which means that the security system is also offline.

Lifting my wristwatch up to my face, I press a button on the side, changing the view from the time to a map of my environment, representing me as a red dot in the center. My eyes search for any white dots, which represent other human life forms. So far, nothing.

Pressing a combination of buttons, I program the watch to vibrate if other life forms come within one hundred feet of me and hide behind a large crate. Sitting down on the ground, I know I might have to wait for a while.

In the corner of the map, I can see my heart rate has spiked up to one hundred and twenty-one beats per minute, and so I begin the Praetorian breathing exercise.

Breathe in for two seconds, hold for two, exhale for two, pause for two. Within minutes, my heart rate has gone down to eighty-four.

Not great, but a lot better.

I take comfort in the fact that if my pulse ever goes to zero, I will at least be avenged. Although I never liked the sub-dermal implant that monitors my vitals, I know that if it fails to ping the Protectorate system every few minutes, it would be assumed that I have died. Once that happens, Praetorians will descend onto my last known location.

I try to banish the thought from my mind. I will not die; I will live.

What would I actually do with my extended life? Would I try to rise to the top, becoming head Praetorian? Do I have political aspirations? The mayor is getting older, and he used to be a Praetorian before being put in charge of Carrington and thus the Protectorate.

I’m not sure if I want to lead the Protectorate, as the mayor does, but I am keenly aware that at no point before have I thought past the age of thirty. I did not need to; that problem had been taken care of for me. Now that living longer is a possibility, I feel overwhelmed by the uncertainty.

Do I even want to live that long? Would it not be better to just live out my life until thirty? Right now, I have ten more years, which I might lose if I get killed on this mission or during the infiltration.

I hate the mayor for putting me in this situation, knowing that even if I wanted to refuse the mission, it was never truly an option.

He was kind enough to present it as an opportunity, but I can see it for what it really is.

An order.

Why me? Are there no other agents more qualified, no people who have more experience?

A wave of despair washes over me as I remember what the mayor said: the life span will be lowered to twenty-eight. I do not even have ten years.

My heart rate jumps twenty points as I feel my wristwatch vibrate.

* * *

_Augustus_

“V, can you hear me?”

No response. I touch the auditory implant near my temple.

“Augustus, you know that touching the implant doesn’t do anything, right?” I can hear V say in a sardonic tone.

This is not the time for an argument, so I choose to ignore the comment. “This place is like a maze. I’m now in a corridor; I can see three doors to the right, and a large one to the left. Which one do I take?”

V pauses for a moment, likely consulting his map. “You’re nearly there. Take the large door on the left.”

Without making a sound, I walk over to the door.

“Are you sure there aren’t any Praetorians in here?”

“Yes, I’m positive,” V replies. “We were able to bribe the guards. The building is offline, and the security is down; I made sure of that. All you need to do is walk through that door, open the large yellow crate, and take as much explosive as you can inconspicuously carry in your backpack.”

I tap the panel next to the door and wait for it to open. Nothing happens.

Right, no power.

Reaching into my coat, I pull out a thin metal tool and slide it into the side of the door, prying it open and stepping inside the main storage room.

“V, you said the container is yellow?”

“Yes, it’s a large yellow container. It should be the only one in there.”

I see it.

Walking over to it, I set down my backpack.

“Will it open, with the facility being shut down?”

“Yes, each container has its own battery. Press the panel on the container to wake it up, and then type in the code: nine-three-five-five.”

The code now typed in, the crate opens with a quiet hiss.

“I’m in.”

A man’s voice booms across the room. “You are in violation of curfew.”

I freeze.

“Turn around.”

Lifting my hands, I slowly turn in place to see the Praetorian with his Atomizer aimed at me.

As I reveal my face to the Praetorian, I notice that he twitches slightly.

Why did he do that? Is he afraid?

Impossible.

He has the law on his side, as well as a very powerful weapon. My Atomizer is in my jacket; I didn’t think I’d need to have it in hand. Even if I did, I would have still been taken by surprise.

As the Praetorian takes a step towards me, his face is partially revealed in the light of the synth-moon.

I begin to laugh. I now know why he twitched. I recognise the Praetorian; he was at the speakeasy earlier today. That’s why he twitched; he must have recognised me too.

“Oh, hello again. I didn’t know Praetorians attended speakeasies.”

“Running facial recognition now,” V whispers in my ear.

Good. With a bit of luck, we might have him.

Did I luck out? If this law enforcer attends bars, maybe this is my way out.

“Explain yourself, citizen. You are violating curfew, as well as trespassing onto a Protectorate facility.”

My eyes narrow. Why has the Praetorian not called for backup yet? Why is he asking questions?

“Who turned us in? Was it your colleagues, the ones we paid off?”

The Praetorian goes silent. Should I not have brought that up? Did they not know about the bribe?

“If I were you, I wouldn’t dig a deeper hole than the one you’re already in. Bribing a Praetorian carries a very long sentence.”

I laugh. “We both already know I’ll be sentenced to death just for being here. How did you find me?”

“The lights were off.”

“We didn’t have enough time,” V says in my ear. “I intended on leaving the lights on, while also disabling the security system, but we would have missed our window.”

“Tell me,” I ask, “why have you not called for backup yet?”

Smiling, I continue, “A Praetorian who attends speakeasies on his time off, and who does not call for backup when he has me with my hands up.”

The situation is becoming stranger by the moment. He definitely should have called for backup by now; that’s what I’ve been told. The first thing a Praetorian does when making an arrest is make a call. Was I given incorrect information?

Impossible, the source is rock solid.

Is there something at play here? Did the Praetorian intend to kill me, without making an arrest? Is he being given orders to do so right now?

I consider whether I should break the cyanide capsule in my mouth, and go out my own way, before the Praetorian has a chance to take me out, or worse, take me in.

If I have to go tonight, at least I will seize the one last shred of freedom I have, deciding when I will die.

Thinking for a moment, I continue, “Can it be that you’re looking to strike a deal?”

“Offering to bribe a Praetorian carries a long sentence.”

“Yes, yes. I know. I’m likely already dead, so what does it matter?”

The real danger is getting stunned. If I bite the capsule, I’d get away; however, if captured, a fate worse than death may await me.

The Praetorian can pull the trigger at any moment, but I do not know which setting his Atomizer is on.

“What do you intend to do with these explosives?” the Praetorian asks.

“Why are you asking? Isn’t that the job of a torturer?”

“The Protectorate does not use torture.”

Laughing, I say, “Oh, I thought you were one of the smart ones. Of course you do.”

“What do you intend to do with the explosives?” The Praetorian asks once more.

“We wish to leave. The Protectorate won’t allow us to, so we intend to make our own way out.”

What do I have to lose?

A drop of sweat slides down my forehead. The Praetorian is likely wondering why I offered this piece of information. Luckily for him, I can not read his mind.

“That would release nuclear fallout into the Protectorate, killing all of us,” says the Praetorian.

“That is a lie. There is no nuclear fallout.”

Why does the Praetorian hesitate? Is he considering not arresting me? Am I hoping this is true? Is there a chance I can bring him over to our side? The skillset of a second Praetorian would be incredibly useful, even a green one like him.

“How do you know?” the Praetorian asks.

Spotting a potential lifeline, I smile. Maybe I’m not going to die tonight after all.

“We have proof of this.”

“Where?” the Praetorian shouts.

“Do you also have a desire to leave?”

The Praetorian does not answer.

“Look, I know you do. You haven’t called for backup, and I’ve seen you drinking at the speakeasy. You’re an A-class, and yet you clearly want to escape your life. We want the same thing.”

My heart rate shoots up. Do I have him?

The Praetorian lowers his Atomizer.

“And if I let you go, what would I have to do to come with you?”

Sighing in relief, I lower my hands. I can always spot someone like this. Some Praetorians must yearn for freedom, too.

“Listen, kid. I’m twenty-nine. In less than a year, I’ll be sacrificed.”

Taking a breath, I continue, “I want to get out, and so do other people. If you wish to join us, we need to make sure we can trust you.”

“What do I need to do?” says the Praetorian.

Can we trust him? What if they know about the mission and sent him here on purpose to try to infiltrate the rebellion?

“I got him,” says V.

Lifting my hand, I point to my eye. “Retinal implant,” I say. “Our entire conversation has been recorded. We know who you are, A-376.”

“Tell him I say hi,” says V.

Laughing, I add, “V says hi.”

I’m not sure if we can trust him or not, but now I know we can blackmail him. We have his identity, footage of our conversation, and V can likely source video of him at the bar as well.

“Do you want us to trust you?” I start. “First, you’d need to let us leave with the explosives. Second, A-376, we know where you live. V has been looking you up during our conversation. Whether you like it or not, you’re now in this. The question is, can we trust you?”

“My name is Adam,” the Praetorian says, as he holsters his Atomizer. “How is that for a first step?”

“Augustus,” I reply, breathing more easily. We now have him admitting to taking a name. This kid is either a burgeoning rebel through and through or incredibly stupid.

“Good,” I continue, my shoulders relaxing further. “Leave, and never speak of this to your superiors. One of us will be in touch soon, and we’ll see if you can be trusted.”

* * *

_Adam_

Turning around, I exit the facility, leaving the rebels to take all the explosives they need. As I step outside, I allow the cool air to soothe my face.

I am ecstatic. I’ve done it! I have managed to get my foot in the door, even if it’s because they believe me to be blackmailable. Once contacted by one of their associates, I’ll have the opportunity to prove myself and gain further access to the organisation.

How would I need to prove myself, I wonder. I will likely have to do something illegal, demonstrating my desire to leave. The mayor has guaranteed me immunity from any crimes I may commit during his infiltration, so it does not matter. Soon, they will all be in custody, and one day, I will die a very old man.
