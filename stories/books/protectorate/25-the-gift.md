# The Gift
### Adam

My feet drag behind the two guards as they carry me through the corridor, throwing me into my cell. Throughout the entire process, the force field never went down; it was up when they dragged me in, and it is still up as they walk out.

Interesting. A one-way forcefield. I can go in, but not out. What allows the guards to leave?

I climb onto the cot and stare at the ceiling, lost in thought, trying to ignore the pain.

Given the circumstances, I did well. I didn’t tell them anything of use, no matter how badly they tortured me. The Praetorian training has proven to be rather useful over the past few days.

I know that there is no chance we will be allowed to stay here, so why would I tell them anything? In fact, there is a good chance we will be sent back, and so, I figure that the less I tell them, the better.

As I sit up on my cot, I can hear footsteps echoing through the hallway.

There’s no way a man made those sounds.

Is there a child in here?

A figure stops next to my cell for a moment before entering. The person in front of me holds a small pouch, looking at me through long, flowing hair.

“Hello?” I ask, unsure of what I am looking at.

The creature resembles the brothers I have seen earlier that day, although it is fully clothed, and its belly is not swollen. My eyes move upwards to its chest, and I notice that those bumps are also present, although smaller than those the brothers in the pods had.

Without a word, the creature places the pouch on the cot before sitting next to me.

“What are you?” I ask.

“What am I?” it echoes, irritated. “What do you mean, what?”

“I meant no offense,” I say, “but I’ve never seen a brother like you before.”

The creature’s eyes widen with surprise and then narrow. “Are there no women where you’re from? I’m a her, a she; not an it,” she spits in a biting tone.

Wo-men?

“I’m sorry, no, you are the first woman I have ever met.”

Earlier today, in the pods, were those also women?

She stares at me, at first with confusion, and then with pity. She reaches for her pouch, taking out a clean cloth and a small bottle. She pours a liquid onto the cloth and rinses it on my sheets.

The woman takes my arm and begins cleaning my wounds, making me wince in pain.

“Stay still,” she says, pulling on my arm. She continues to dab my cuts and bruises as I look at her. Suddenly, the realization washes over me.

I have seen her before, at the end of fever dreams, moments before waking up. I have seen this before, her shoulders moving rhythmically, causing me pain, although that was not the entire truth. She is cleaning my wounds, helping me.

These events have happened before, although only in my mind; however, it is now clear to me that I lacked context. The thought of my second vision came into mind, where she is running towards me, arms outstretched.

I know that it is her, the same creature-woman I have seen twice over, but I’m not sure what to make of it. I know that I have already misinterpreted the first vision, so is it possible I am misinterpreting the second as well?

I decide to put the matter aside for now, as worrying does not help. I will find out what the context is at the appropriate time, hopefully a pleasant surprise, like this one.

My eyes begin to wander, examining the features of the woman in front of me, and I decide that I like her very much. I feel the urge to get closer to her, but something tells me that she would disapprove.

Her long, dark blonde hair follows the motions of her head as she moves and turns, examining my skin and cleaning it.

“My name is Adam. Do you have a name?” I ask. She ignores me, continuing to do her work.

Is she angry with me? I truly am sorry about not knowing she is a woman, but how could I have known? I have never seen one before today.

“Why are you helping me?” I continue. This time, she stops, her eyes turning to me. I can sense that she does not like me very much, although I’m not entirely sure why.

“Because I’ve been told to,” she replies, lowering her eyes back onto her work.

“Are you a medic or a nurse?” I ask.

“Neither,” she replies. “I am a dress.”

“A dress?” I echo, my eyebrows lifting in confusion.

“Do you not know anything?” she snaps at me.

“I’m afraid I’m not from around here,” I say, leaning back against the cold wall.

“That I can see,” she mutters, getting back to the work of scrubbing my wounds.

“I’m truly sorry if I have offended you,” I say. “I know nothing about your customs, and I am curious. If you wish to be left alone, I will be quiet.”

I close my eyes. I find that this makes it easier to bear the pain, not just the physical kind, but also the feeling that this woman, whom I very much like, seems to hate me intensely.

“Dresses are women who are born and bred to become Hosts,” she says. I open my eyes and look at her. Sensing the next question, she continues, “Hosts are women who enter the Life Tanks, producing soldiers for the High Ones.”

“Earlier today,” I start, “my companions and I came across a structure with what I now know to be women, held in medical pods.”

Her eyes shoot up at me, but she says nothing.

“Is that what I saw? Hosts?” I ask.

“Yes,” she murmurs, getting back to work.

How many Hosts must the High Ones have? I saw eight in that one building, but given that they are unguarded, that means there must be more dotted around the landscape. How many? Dozens? Hundreds? Thousands? I closed my eyes, trying not to imagine such horrors.

“I’m sorry,” I say to her. The woman’s hands stop for a moment as my words reach her.

“It’s not your fault.”

It would seem like a lack of choice is not exclusively the domain of the Protectorate.

As her hands move expertly across my arms, I notice a thin gold ring around her index finger. I want to ask about it, but I have decided I do not want to waste a question. I have managed to get her to at least not openly despise me, and that is progress; progress which I do not want to squander.

“When are you due to—”, I pause, unsure of how to phrase the question.

“Become a Host?” she asks, sighing.

“Yes,” I reply.

“Soon,” she says.

“Oh,” I reply, then go quiet.

“The Life Tank is a rather ironic name for what they put us in,” she says, continuing to work. “Once we’re in, we never come out. They place us in a deep sleep, and we produce children, over and over, until our bodies are no longer useful. When we reach that point, we get—” she hesitates for a moment, before completing her sentence, “discarded.”

So her consciousness will end the moment she enters one of those tanks.

“Our bodies are genetically engineered,” she continues, “much like the High Ones, to live much longer lives than we naturally would.”

“All so that you can produce more children for them,” I say.

“Yes,” she answers.

She looks a little bit younger than me, but can’t be that much younger, maybe by one or two years. How tragic; she is engineered to live longer than the average human lifespan, only to be cut down in her late teens, never to wake up again. Both of our lives are to be ended far too early.

She is destined for the Life Tanks, whilst I will surely be killed sooner rather than later. What does it matter if it is by these people or by the Protectorate?

“Where I’m from, they limit our lifespan to thirty years. Once you reach that age, you have to die, to make room for the next generation,” I say.

“How do you have a next generation, without any women?” she asks.

“Incubators,” I say. “They produce our brothers.”

“They only produce men?” she asks, as she takes out a small container, unscrewing the top. She runs her finger through the ointment inside and begins carefully rubbing it on my wounds.

I think about it for a moment. Why do the incubators only produce men? I had never questioned this before because, well, I was unaware of the existence of women. Why would I question it?

“I suppose,” I say. “What is this stuff?”

“A healing ointment of my own making,” she says, unable to hide her pride. “I’m pretty good with plants.”

The ointment smells nice, and I find her touch soothing. I feel grateful to her, as my mind wanders.

“How many women are there, compared to men?” I find myself wondering out loud.

“About half and half,” replies the woman. “Usually slightly more women than men.”

My head shoots up as the realization hits me.

Half.

“What’s wrong?” she cries.

I can feel my breathing accelerating as I engage in the Praetorian exercise to bring it back down to normal.

“Many years ago,” I say, bringing my breathing under control, “something happened to our population; it was cut in half.” I pause, gathering my thoughts, “I don’t know why, at least not yet, but I suppose it is possible the women died out.”

“I’m sorry to hear that,” she whispers, as she leans back. She places the cloth back in her pouch, along with her bottle and ointment, and stands up, making her way back to the corridor.

“My name is Ambrosia, by the way,” she says as she stops for a moment. Without turning, she walks back out through the force field.
