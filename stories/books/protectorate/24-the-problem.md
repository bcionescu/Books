# The Problem
### Samantha, Present Day

I tap my fingers on the armrest of the throne. My throne. Out of the corner of my eye, I can see my mother approaching, solemn and dignified, as she looks up at me.

“How do you find the throne?” Katherine asks.

“I find that it fits me well,” I say.

I do not look at her.

My coronation is to take place tomorrow, merely a formality at this point, as it is customary for a new Imperatrix to take on responsibilities as early as a few months before the event.

I look at Katherine with disdain in my heart, as I wonder how long she has known. How long have you known, mother, that you never passed on the gift to me? When did you first realize that you robbed me of my birthright?

Turning my head, I resume looking into the distance, as I toy with the golden ring on my finger.

I’ve long suspected that I did not have the gift, but I was never quite certain. After all, how can you know if you have it or not? I can’t step into the frame of mind of someone else, and so, I could not have known for certain.

When I looked at someone, I could sense things, such as whether they might be lying, or whether they liked me or not, but nothing more.

I was unsure if this was the gift until a few days ago. As part of my upcoming mandate, which I am to somehow serve for the next twenty years, I have been entrusted with more and more secrets.

One of these secrets was The Pact, negotiated before I was even born. I watched the recordings, featuring my mother, grandmother, and the two men from the Protectorate.

This is what gave me confirmation, certainty of the crime that she had committed against me. During the negotiations, I watched the motions of the women during the negotiations.

I realized that there were no discussions between them, merely long pauses, and nothing else. This is when I understood what was happening; they were peering into each other’s minds.

There was no need for words, only thoughts, which they could relay to each other. Knowing this made me understand that I do not possess such a gift, and yet, everyone expects me to.

What am I to do? I think of Katherine, telling me of the weight that a monarch must bear, mostly alone, and I shudder.

Without the gift, what am I to do?

“Have you decided how you will handle our guests?” Katherine asks as she sits down on the steps in front of the throne, facing away from me.

My eyes turn to her. Why have you done this to me, mother?

“The Pact specifies that if someone from the Protectorate is caught outside, they are to be returned promptly,” I say, reciting the clause from the Pact word for word.

While I may not have received the gift, I have been blessed, or, some might say, cursed, with a perfect memory.

“Correct,” Katherine says.

I continue to fidget with my ring, lost in thought.

“Who is to say what promptly means?” I ask. “And how are they to know that we have them?”

Katherine thinks for a few moments before answering, “It is largely up to you to decide, of course. Regarding the second problem, there is always the possibility that they were sent here on purpose, to see if we return them quickly, or if we in fact return them all.”

She is mocking me. We both know that there is an easy way for me to solve this problem; I have them brought here, and I peer into their minds. This would reveal the truth, and we would know. However, I am unable to do that. If I had them brought here, I would look at them and learn nothing more than anyone else would.

“Such matters are of little importance,” I say. “They will be questioned, and those who survive will be returned to the Protectorate when I see fit. If they were sent here on purpose, I dare them to do something about it.”

This is the only option available to me. What else can I do?

“It’s been a long time since you negotiated the Pact,” I continue. “There has been little communication since. I’m curious to see what has changed in the intervening years.”

My mother says nothing as she sways her body, looking out into the distance.

“You liked him, didn’t you?” I say, as I look away.

“Who?” my mother asks.

“The envoy from the Protectorate, you know which one.”

Despite her facing away from me, I can see the edges of a smile.

She did like him.

“Is he my father?” I ask.

“No,” she replies. “Your father was a donor, genetically matched with me, as is proper.”

I know she’s telling the truth. I don’t need special abilities to tell that much.

My thoughts turn to the coming years ahead of me. How am I to survive this? I have no choice; I must hide myself from the world. People will assume that I can see into their minds, and so, I must seem distant and calculated.

If they have done something wrong, and I punish them, they will assume that I know, hence why they are being punished. However, if no punishment comes, they will assume weakness.

This will mean getting rid of good people often, in the off chance that they might be plotting something against me. Every time I send a good woman or man to their death, people will assume that I know something they don’t, thus allowing me to keep my power.

I feel tired. I’m barely twenty years old, and already I feel like crawling under a rock and sleeping for years.

“Did you ever find out what happened to him?” I ask.

“Who?” my mother inquires.

“The man from the Protectorate,” I clarify.

“No,” she says, sadness filling her voice.

This time, I know she is lying.
