# The Infiltration
### Adam

Dressed in civilian clothes, I make my way through Invictus Park. Wrapping my jacket tightly around me, I can feel my Atomizer in its hidden holster, making me feel safe.

It’s set to stun, figuring that no matter what happens, that would be the smartest way forward. Even if I am forced to blow my cover, taking them in alive is better, as we might be able to extract some information from them.

I think about what Augustus said in the Protectorate facility, about torture, and I shake the thought away. I wish GG could be with me, but no such luck; he’d be too conspicuous, plus, he’d probably try to shock everyone in sight.

Smiling to myself, I near the statue. Three men stand by it, talking casually. While I do not recognise one of the men, I do spot Augustus and Sylvan.

I approach the men. “Good evening.”

At the sound of my voice, they turn around to meet me.

“Evening, Adam,” says Sylvan. “You’ve already met Augustus.”

He pats the tall man next to him on the back.

“Hello again,” Augustus says, extending his hand towards me. I reach for his hand and shake it.

“This is V,” Sylvan continues, pointing to a shorter, skinnier man.

“Greetings,” V says, looking up at me.

Sylvan turns around and points to an inconspicuous building.

“— and _that_ is our mission.”

I look at the building, but I can not make out its purpose. It does not look like a residential building, but it also does not have any signs on it, meaning that it could not be a government building.

“What is it?”

“The Protectorate Archive,” answers Augustus.

“That building houses secrets, documents, and books that the Protectorate does not want the public to know about,” says Sylvan. “We believe many of them date from before the wars, or the event that caused our ancestors to go underground.”

“We know,” V interrupts, “that essentially all of those documents have been digitized, and are stored along with the hard copies.”

“Yes,” says Sylvan. “It would be easier for us to exfiltrate the digital data, rather than physical copies. That is why V is here.”

V waves at me, smirking.

“Why is Augustus here?” I ask.

“He’s the muscle. He’ll ensure that no guards give us difficulty for long. You,” says Sylvan, pointing his finger at me, “are here to help us navigate the building; your Praetorian wristwatch and your training will come in handy.”

“And you?” I say, looking at Sylvan.

Sylvan’s mouth turns upwards into a smile as he removes the Atomizer from his jacket. “My job,” he says, “is to stand behind you at all times, and make sure you don’t try to turn on us.”

“Sounds like you have the easiest job out of all of us,” I say, smiling back.

“We’ll see,” replies Sylvan. “Alright, let’s move out. Adam, you take the lead.”

Bringing my wrist nearer to my face, I press a button on my watch, revealing a map of Invictus Park with myself in the middle, and three dots surrounding me.

“First,” I say, “we should walk around the building and see if we can spot a way to access the roof. All government buildings have such an entry point for maintenance reasons. The building has only three floors, so climbing up would not be difficult.”

Augustus pulls out four tubular devices. “We’ve already thought of that.”

“Grappling guns?” I ask.

“Yup,” Augustus grins.

“That’s not a good idea,” I say.

Augustus’s face drops. “Why not?”

“Many government facilities have semi-automatic security systems in place, where lifeform activity is monitored. If aberrant patterns are detected, a silent alarm is triggered.”

“Such as someone suddenly appearing on the roof after sliding up the edge of the building,” says Sylvan.

“Precisely,” I reply.

Augustus curses and hides the grappling guns.

“Why did you not disable the building, as you did with the construction warehouse?” I ask.

“To extract the digitized information, the power has to be on,” replies V.

“So, to steal the information, the security systems have to be on. On top of that, we need to make sure that we dodge any Praetorians, which is where I come in.”

“Correct,” Sylvan nods.

“What about the security cameras?” I ask.

“That’s where I come in,” says V. “To not attract attention to the building, the Protectorate did not install any cameras outside; however, there are plenty inside. There is a maintenance control panel on the roof. Once near it, I can plug into the system and rig the cameras, looping the last few seconds seamlessly. This way, the Praetorian monitoring the cameras will not notice us in the hallways.”

“Won’t our presence on the roof trigger an alarm?” asks Augustus.

“As long as we use the service ladder, it will not, as our presence there would be seen as normal, typical of maintenance staff. As long as we don’t do anything strange, like appear out of nowhere, or go through a window, we’ll be invisible to the automated security system,” I say.

Oh, no.

My stomach sinks as I recognize the sound.

“Quick, hide.” I run towards the tree line.

“What?” asks Augustus.

“Hide,” I cry.

The men follow me and jump into a cluster of bushes. Moments later, a Lumina appears, hovering over where we last stood, rotating in place.

“Don’t move,” I whisper. “It’s less likely to spot you that way.”

All four of us hold our breath as our eyes fixate on the drone.

This is not good.

I desperately try not to breathe.

This Lumina might blow my cover. I can try to shoot it down, but when it fails to report anything via a relay, an automated alarm will be triggered.

I decide that we have to stay still until its pre-programmed algorithm decides to abandon its search, and move onto a different target.

After what felt like an eternity, the Lumina shoots up and disappears. We exhale at the same time and begin taking deep breaths in.

Augustus starts to laugh and pats me on the back.

We stand. “Good job, kid.”

“You did well,” says V, as Sylvan nods his head at me in approval.

“Thanks. Let’s get back to the mission.”

I take out my wristwatch and check it as we approach the building.

“There are currently no Praetorians patrolling outside, and none near the windows; at least on this side of the building.”

“V?” Sylvan asks.

“Yes, boss,” he replies, blinking twice.

“What are you doing?” I ask.

“Black market night vision implant,” says Sylvan. “Quite the mouthful, no?”

“So he can see in the dark?”

“Yes. It’s an older model, so we don’t get any extra data, but it does the job, and it allows him to see in the dark and zoom in. V, can you see an access point on this side of the building?”

V pauses for a moment, his eyes moving around erratically. As he zooms in and focuses, his pupils dilate and contract.

“Yes,” says V. “There is a ladder leading up to the roof near the far edge of the building.”

“Alright, let’s go then,” says Sylvan.

We begin walking to the edge of the facility, making sure to stay in the park, well hidden by the trees and the darkness.

“V, can I ask you a question?”

“Depends on what the question is.”

“When Augustus broke into the facility, how were you able to help him remotely? When Lumina drones gather data, they need to first make their way to a relay before the information is collected at a central point; however, you were able to help him from a distance. How?”

“I was in a building across the street. The range was short enough for me to be able to communicate with Augustus and gather data. I was able to see your exchange through the large windows of the facility.”

My assumption that V was far away was incorrect. If I had taken Augustus down, V would have been able to snipe me with ease.

I was careless.

“Ok,” starts Sylvan. “Adam, from this moment onward, I’ll need you to keep your eyes on your wristwatch and let us know if you see anyone getting close. V, until we get to the ladder, I’ll need you to keep an eye on the windows and make sure nobody can see us.”

We both nod in agreement as I tap a few buttons on my watch, requesting that I be notified if anyone gets too close to us, just in case.

We reach the outer wall of the facility without being spotted and begin climbing the ladder, with me going first.

I can feel my hands shaking.

As we slowly make our way up, my wristwatch buzzes, freezing me in place and causing Sylvan to bump into me.

“What’s the matter?” Sylvan whispers.

Looking down, I indicate that we should be quiet. Sylvan relays the motion to the two below. Having made the mistake of looking down, I close my eyes and steady myself. Why did people have to build things so high off the ground?

There is someone on the roof, likely a Praetorian. My eyes fixate on the moving dot, and I watch with bated breath as it moves around.

My hands grip onto the steel bar of the ladder as the dot nears the edge of the building.

I can feel my heart racing.

What should I do? Looking up, I can see the edges of the guard’s uniform. If he looks down, we’re dead.

A cigarette butt falls onto my head as I stifle a curse. Looking at my wristwatch, I can see the dot moving towards the center of the roof, and I hear a door creak as the guard disappears inside the building.

Breathing a little easier, we resume our climb. As we reach the roof, V rushes over to the control panel and connects it to a handheld device via a cable.

“I’ll need a few minutes.”

I wander back near the service ladder and try looking over the ledge, my stomach turning, my head spinning. All things considered, the mission is going relatively smoothly. We had a close encounter with the guard earlier, but we managed to get away unscathed.

My muscles feel tense. I stretch my arms out and place my hands on the parapet, while V works on rigging the cameras.

As my hand touches the concrete, I feel a strange object. I pick it up, and my heart sinks as I bring it into the moonlight.

“Guys?” I whisper.

“Huh?” answers Sylvan.

Walking over to the others, I show them the item. It is a lighter, undoubtedly the guard’s, one which he will very likely come back to collect.

“Damn it,” Sylvan says, as the door opens, bringing us face to face with the Praetorian.

I am the closest.

I have no choice.

Sprinting over to him, I place my hand over his mouth and wrestle him to the ground. In the chaos that follows, I manage to grab my Atomizer, and I pull the trigger.

I instantaneously fall to the floor as the man beneath me disappears. I now lie in a pool of blood.

“What did you do?” Sylvan whispers, traces of panic in his voice.

“I—I had it set to _stun_,” I manage to murmur.

Everyone stares at me, not knowing what to do.

“It is clear,” says V, “that in the commotion, the guard tried to grab your Atomizer and accidentally switched it to _vaporize_ as he did so.”

Regaining his composure, Sylvan nods his head in agreement.

“You’re definitely one of us now, kid. There’s no going back.”

In my heart, I know this to be true. Even though the mayor said he would forgive any crime I would commit while undercover, I know that grace likely does not extend to murder.

I look at my hands, covered in another man’s blood. I am now a murderer; no better than the mayor, or the system that he props up.

Picking up the dead man’s uniform, I can see his name etched into its breast, “A-384,” I murmur.

The Praetorian had been younger than me.

“Get up,” says Augustus, as he grabs me and helps me stand up. “You’re alright, kid, it isn’t your fault, do you understand?”

I nod, blood covering my entire body.

“V, how’re the cameras?” asks Sylvan.

“Almost there, boss,” V responds.

“And you,” adds Sylvan, “make sure you set your Atomizer back to _stun_. We don’t want any more bodies.”

Looking down at my weapon, I make the required change.

“The camera feed is now looped,” says V. “The Praetorians monitoring the feeds are being shown seamless thirty-second repeats of the same clips, over and over.”

As the realization hits, my legs begin to feel weak.

“Oh, no,” I say, looking at the other men.

“What?” asked Sylvan.

“All Praetorian vitals are monitored; not in real time, but close enough. The system pings our chips every few minutes, retrieving data such as heart rate and blood oxygen levels.”

“So when the system will ping this poor soul,” V says, as he picks up the dead man’s chip from the puddle of blood, “there will be no answer.”

“Correct,” I say, my voice shaking. “Which means that in a few minutes, a silent alarm will be triggered, sending Praetorians our way, and alerting all the ones already here.”

Sylvan takes a deep breath and thinks for a few moments.

“V, if we abandon the mission, what are the odds that we’ll be able to succeed later?”

“Very low,” V says, “as they will likely increase the level of security, or move the archive altogether.”

“We agree, then,” Sylvan replies. “We’ll just have to be quick; no more stealth. Adam, how far can you extend the search area of your watch?”

“Enough to cover the whole building,” I say, “but the wider the area is, the less accurate each dot becomes. If I were to extend it that far, lifesigns would jump around, lag, and some would disappear, and come back minutes later.”

“Would you be able to tell us how many Praetorians are in this building, not counting the ones that will soon be on their way?”

Operating my wristwatch with blood-covered hands is difficult, but I manage to press the right buttons and increase the surface area of the scan.

“There are five more guards in the building. Three patrolling around, and two more in this room here,” I say as I indicate it on the map.

“That is the room we’re trying to get to,” says Sylvan. “Ok, so we head there, and we stun the guards. If we encounter anyone else on the way there, we drop them immediately. We don’t have any time left for stealth.”

“V, can you get back into that terminal and figure out how far the nearest Praetorians are?” asks Sylvan.

V runs back to the terminal. “On it.”

“Adam, if the alarm does sound, will you be notified?”

I think about it for a moment.

“Theoretically, yes. As I am in the vicinity, my watch should be pinged.”

“Great,” replies Sylvan. “It looks like this is your last night as Praetorian. There’s no turning back now.”

He is right. Once my watch ID gets tied to this location via the silent alarm, I’m done.

It is only a matter of time.

“The nearest Praetorian vehicle, containing up to four men, is seven minutes away. Assuming they don’t move, that is how long we have until they get here, once the alarm gets triggered.”

“Ok,” says Sylvan. “Everyone, check your weapons; we’re heading in.”

As I move towards the door, Sylvan puts his hand on my shoulder.

“No, there’s no need for you to go first now; no more stealth. Plus, I no longer need to worry about you now. You can hang in the back. Augustus,” he says, turning his head to him, “you take the lead.”

“Got it.” Augustus moves towards the door, Atomizer in hand.

V disconnects his device from the maintenance terminal and joins the group.

“Let’s go,” says Augustus, as he opens the door.

The inside of the building looks very different from the outside. Every surface is coated in black, except for the floor, which lights up as it detects our weight.

“This is not good,” says Sylvan. “The floor might give away our position. V, can you do something about this?”

“If I had a few hours, maybe. As things stand, we don’t have that long.”

Sylvan swears and indicates that we should keep moving.

As we walk down what seems like an endless corridor, the floors illuminate, betraying our presence. Nearing the door to the data storage room, I am grateful that we have yet to encounter any other guards.

We flank the door on each side, as Sylvan counts us down.

_1-2-3_

Augustus swears. The door is locked.

Sylvan’s eyes widen.

A voice rings out behind the door. “Who’s there?”

Everyone goes quiet. Sylvan turns, indicating that we should abandon the mission.

Before anyone can follow, I rush to the door. “It’s A-384. Sorry, sir, I went out for a smoke and lost my key card.”

“Again?” The guard approaches the door.

“I swear, new kid, if you do this again, I’ll write you up.”

As the guard unlocks the door from the other side, Augustus’s Atomizer drops him, and we rush inside.

“Where’s the other guard?” I ask, checking my wrist watch. “The device indicates an extra life form in the room.”

“There is no other guard—”, says V.

“Who’s A-384?” interrupts Sylvan.

“The Praetorian on the roof,” I murmur.

I feel sick.

Sylvan nods, understanding. “You did well, kid.”

“Come here, Adam,” continues V, as he walks towards a black glass wall. “Look inside.”

Straining my eyes, I can see a medical pod, generally used for automated surgery. Inside it is something, or someone.”

V touches a panel, and an opening appears in the wall.

“Follow me.”

As we approach the medical pod, I can see inside it more clearly.

“Is that a person?” I ask.

“Somewhat. We supposedly come from incubators; you, me, Sylvan, all of us. However, that doesn’t mean that every attempt is successful. In some instances, a human is created, but they are not fully formed. This poor brother has no brain and no soul. He is, in essence, a human-shaped grouping of cells.”

I look upon him, trying to suppress my disgust. He has three eyes and extra arms, if they can be called that.

“What is he doing here?” I ask as V searches for something in his tool belt.

“This is the data storage unit.”

My eyes widen.

“We don’t have time, so I’ll keep it brief. If you want to store information, how do you do that?” he asks rhetorically, as he finds what he was looking for: a syringe.

“People used to keep information on stone tablets, and then eventually, paper. However, as our data storage needs grew, we started storing data on hard drives and then in solid-state storage. This worked well enough, but they had a fatal flaw.”

V pauses and taps a few buttons. The pod opens with a hiss.

“You see,” he continues, as he starts to look for a vein, “storage technology is vulnerable to something called a bit flip. Data is stored in binary, as ones and zeros, each taking up one bit. On occasion, cosmic rays that make their way to Earth can flip one of these bits, corrupting data. There is no way to shield from this.”

V pauses for a moment, having found a vein, and begins to draw blood. “As it turns out, encoding data into DNA is a lot more reliable. It’s still stored in binary, ones and zeros, but it is immune to cosmic rays; however, it is susceptible to mutation.”

The vial is halfway full.

“That being said, if you place the subject in suspended animation, such as in this case here, you can store data for centuries, with minimal risk of corruption. This man is likely as old as the Protectorate.”

V looks at the vial and says to Sylvan, “The first one is complete. Getting started on the second.”

He places the first vial in his belt and takes out a second one, plugging it into the syringe and continuing to draw blood.

“So the data is stored in this poor creature, a failed brother?”

“Yes,” confirms V. “The data is stored in the blood cells’ genomes. The downside is that we don’t know exactly what we’re extracting; however, three vials should give us a mountain of data to look through, assuming they’re kept stable at the right temperature.”

I look on as the vial slowly fills with blood.

“Grab one of the cryo-cases off the wall there. Make sure it’s charged.”

I begin to walk towards the cases when my wristwatch beeps. I look at it in despair as I receive confirmation that the Praetorians are on their way.

“They’re coming!” I cry.

I rush to the wall and grab the first container I see.

* * *

### Augustus

Sylvan slaps my shoulder. “Take the lead.”

Placing my ear to the door, I listen for incoming guards. Looking down at my Atomizer, I make sure it is set to stun.

Looking to my right, I can see V and the new boy panicking. V curses as he removes the syringe.

“Only a vial and a half.”

“That’ll have to do,” says Sylvan. “Let’s go!“

I look over at Adam as he examines his watch. He looks like he is about to pass out.

“The other guards,” Adam says, “they’re coming too!”

“Of course, they’d also be alerted,” says Sylvan. “Never mind, let’s go. Back to the roof.”

V throws the vials into the cryo-case and awaits further instructions. I signal to everyone to get ready, as I open the door and rush into the corridor.

I see the guard aim his Atomizer at the kid. Jumping between them, I aim at the guard and smash the trigger on my Atomizer. The Praetorian must have fired at the same time, as we are both on the floor, inert, the other man facing the wall.

Is this what being stunned feels like?

It’s not that bad.

I can see the kid looking at me, pale and scared.

It’s alright, kid, there are worse things in this world.

My thoughts wander back a few weeks, as the doctor gave me my diagnosis. What a joke, I am to die before even reaching thirty.

This is the best possible outcome. At least, this way, my death means something. The kid gave me an extra couple of days, recalling how I was ready to bite down on the cyanide capsule when we first met.

I would laugh if I could.

I can feel my body being turned to face Sylvan.

Oh yes, old friend. You know what you need to do. I’m glad that it will be you who does it.

I can’t move my eyes, but I can still see the kid. He is saying something as Sylvan switches his Atomizer from _stun_ to _vaporize_.

It’s not such a bad day to die.

As good as any other, I suppose.

* * *

### Adam

I find myself shaking Sylvan. “Why did you do that?”

My cheeks feel damp.

“There’s no time, kid. Let’s go, now!”

The three of us remaining fly up the stairs and make it onto the roof.

“Adam, what’s the status?”

Still shaking, I check my wristwatch. Still set to far range scan, it looks like most of the Praetorians are near the front entrance.

“Front of the building,” I whisper, as I adjust my watch to near range. “Nothing in front of us,” I continue, as we head towards the ladder and down to the ground.

This is not the time to mourn, but to live. I need to think of anything other than the fact that Augustus has just given his life for me.

I need to be anywhere else but here.

“There,” Sylvan says, as he points to a small clearing between the trees.

My eyes narrow, but I can not make out anything other than trees. Sylvan rushes towards the spot and jumps, disappearing in mid-air.

I want to stop, but I’m pushed from behind by V. “Go, go!” he whispers, pushing me into the clearing.

Suddenly, I hit a hard metal wall.

I am inside a large vehicle.

Behind me, the building disappears from view as the door closes silently.

Sylvan and V strap themselves in their seats.

“Get in a chair,” Sylvan hisses.

I crawl into the seat behind V.

“Where’s Augustus?” asks the driver.

“He didn’t make it,” pants Sylvan.

Swearing, the driver taps the controls and says, “Eve, I’m taking over.”

“Understood,” says a warm voice, unlike any that I have ever heard. “Manual driving mode is now enabled.”

Even if I could speak, I would not know what to say. In the driver’s chair, expertly maneuvering around the trees, sits Octavian, my boss and head Praetorian.

Noticing my surprise, Sylvan says, “Relax, kid, he’s one of us. You both are now.”

The memory of my meeting with Octavian the day before floods my mind.

_I do not know whether you can truly be trusted with this._

Is this what he meant? At the time, I had assumed he meant that he was unsure whether I could complete the mission.

It would seem that his question had a far deeper meaning.

“Give your watch to V,” Sylvan says.

I take it off without a word. Using a tool from his belt, V fries it before throwing it out the window.

“Why did you do that?” I ask.

“The thing can likely be used to track you. You’re no longer a Praetorian, but one of us.”

“Can’t they track us anyway, through our chips?” I ask.

“Yes, however, the watch emits a far stronger signal, revealing your identity, as it is tied to you. Our chips simply reveal us as lifeforms, and nothing more. Praetorian chips send more data, but not location data. In the event of death, the location is sourced from your wristwatch, not the chip itself.”

That makes sense.

I look behind me, towards the back of the vehicle. By the wall, I see a similar pod to that in which the creature was kept.

V follows my gaze and says, “Oh, yes. It’s the same stuff, although it’s mobile, so it’s not quite as effective. It can suspend you for a long time if you’re healthy; if dying, it may keep you barely alive, until you can get help.”

“I’ve never seen anything like this in a vehicle before. That voice,” I add, indicating to the control panel, “I’ve never heard anything like it either.”

V smirks, saying, “This vehicle is not available to mere mortals.”

I understand. As I have my perks, so does Octavian.

“Where are we going now?” I ask, knowing that I could never go back home.

“Hideout,” Sylvan answers.

“It’s more than that,” says V, as he lifts the cryo-container to his face, “it’s my home.”

His eyes narrow as he reads the display output on the container and curses.

“3% battery,” he says. He lowers the container back in his lap.

I made a mistake.

“I’m sorry, the alarm went off, and I panicked.”

“Don’t worry about it, kid, you did a lot better than we were expecting you to,” says Sylvan. His head turning towards V, he asks, “Will the battery last until we get to your place?”

“Not likely.”

“What does that mean?” asks Sylvan.

“It means that the blood will not be kept at the correct temperature. The longer that remains the case, the more the data will be corrupted.”

“Can you reconstruct it?” asks Octavian.

“I will try,” says V, “although it’s difficult to say until I can sequence it.”

“Sylvan, why did you vaporise Augustus?” I cry, unable to hold the question in any longer.

“Death’s not that bad, kid. There are things which are worse than death.”

“Such as?”

“Like getting captured and interrogated. I’d expect you to do the same for me,” Sylvan says in a solemn voice.

“He got stunned,” says V. “We would not have been able to carry him with us, and if we left him there, he would have been questioned. You haven’t been a Praetorian long, have you?”

“No,” I mutter.

“That’s alright,” V says, “they haven’t shown you everything yet, isn’t that right, Octavian?”

The head Praetorian drives in silence for a few moments before replying, “When capture is imminent, death is the preferable option.”

“Augustus was a good man,” he continues, “he’s the reason why you’re here. We were debating what to do with you when he intervened on your behalf. He said that he had a good feeling about you, that you are one of us; that we should give you a chance.”

“Looks like he was right,” says Sylvan.

They knew, or at least suspected. Of course, with Octavian being amongst them. They pieced together that I was sent to infiltrate them, and yet, here I am, still alive.

We spend the rest of the drive in silence.
