# Opportunities
### Adam

The entity flashes before me, its long, flowing hair covering much of its face. Its eyes fixed upon mine, mouth curled downwards, arms outstretched, it runs towards me.

Drenched in sweat, I awake in my apartment.

What if something goes wrong during the mission? What if the rebels suspect I am an infiltrator? The mayor has given me an opportunity at long life, but it is becoming clear that this mission may instead severely shorten it.

And then, there is that creature, the brother who is unlike any brother I have ever seen.

It does not matter. I was unable to refuse the mission, even if I wanted to. I need to do my best when the time comes, and hope things go to plan.

Turning my head, I look around my A-class apartment. The lights are on most of the time, I have running water, and—for a few hours per week—I also enjoy warm water. These are many of the privileges afforded to me by my class and profession. Others have far less.

I have read about life before the wars, how brothers had access to limitless energy, thanks to atomics, the same force that destroyed them in the end. My eyes wander to the Atomizer next to my bed, and I wonder why we use the same technology.

Looking out my window, the synth-sky is slightly illuminated, its dark blue color indicating that it is early morning. I can’t sleep, so I throw on my civilian clothes and head out.

The artificial breeze is rather cool today, and the cold cuts through me as I pull my jacket tighter against the chill.

I never understood why the Protectorate had decided to simulate the weather above. Centuries ago, there was a state above ground, called California.

Even though much about the old world has been lost, and documents have become corrupted, my position as a Praetorian affords me information that ordinary citizens do not have access to.

The climate of California was apparently rather mild all year round, although you would get the occasional chill. From the west, the ocean borders it, and mountains demarcate its territory from all other directions. I try to imagine what a mountain would look like, or an ocean, for that matter, but fail to do so.

Why did the Protectorate simulate its weather? Why can it not just be mild at all times? Is there something about human psychology that requires suffering? Is it good for me to ask so many questions? Why can’t I just let it be?

Above my head, I can hear the whir of a Lumina. As it flies by, it stops above me for a moment before continuing on its path. The Lumina is a special kind of drone; spherical-shaped, equipped with cameras covering all directions.

Despite their name, they do not illuminate. In fact, they are quite hard to see, unless you know what to look for. They are programmed to dart around the Protectorate at all times to monitor the behavior of its citizens. Whenever they encounter something that they consider interesting, they stop their movement and hover, scanning the environment and analyzing. Once they get close to a relay, they then transmit the data back to the main console, reporting their findings and, if needed, the Praetorians are notified.

This means that criminals and wrongdoers have a window of time in which they can try to escape before the Lumina reaches a relay. In reality, there is nowhere to run. The drones have everyone’s genetic fingerprint and can identify you in many ways, even if your face is covered.

Your identity can be revealed by your gate, the way in which you move your hands, even your unique aortal signature, or perspiration patterns. Everyone in the Protectorate is monitored from the moment they come out of the incubator.

Looking up, I wonder if the people of California lived similar lives.

Continuing with my walk, I know that I do not need to worry about the Lumina. If I were an ordinary citizen, being out during curfew, as I am right now, would warrant my arrest. However, given my status, no such report will be made. The mayor has his privileges, but so do I.

As I make my way down the streets of Carrington, the synth-Sun rises, shortening my shadow. The previously deserted streets start to look busier as my brothers leave their homes and begin heading to work.

The only people in the protectorate who have vehicles are the Praetorians and the mayor. Everyone else walks everywhere. I wonder if it had always been like this.

The more people appear in the street, the less enjoyable my walk becomes, so I decide to head back to my apartment. On my way home, I see that the speakeasy is open, so I wander in, a small bell announcing my arrival.

I sit down at the bar.

“What will it be?” the bartender asks.

“Shine, please.”

I find the question amusing, given that there is only ever one option. The shortages began years ago, meaning that the only option available is moonshine.

The bartender pours a glass and places it in front of me. I reach into my jacket and drop a coin on the bar.

Bringing the small glass to my lips, I take a swig and wince. I hate the stuff, knowing that it can probably strip the paint off my Atomizer. Why am I here?

I feel nervous. The mission starts in roughly twelve hours, and I need something to soothe my nerves.

To my left, two men are engaged in a private conversation. What would they do if they knew I was a Praetorian? What am I doing in a place like this? No matter, I am not in uniform, and when I am on duty, my face is partially obstructed anyway, so they would not be able to tell.

“Hey, can I help you?” one of the men says.

Damn it, I stared for too long.

“No, not at all. Apologies.”

I turn back towards the bar and cringe as I take another sip.

The man’s eyes linger for a moment before turning back to his friend and continuing their quiet conversation.

I wish they could have music, thinking back to my meeting with the mayor. I had never heard such dulcet tones, and I suddenly find that I miss them.

Seedy establishments such as these often play white noise, as is the case with this one, in an attempt to give their patrons a semblance of privacy.

This is illegal, and as a Praetorian, it is my duty to enforce the law; however, I find that I like privacy—a luxury, living in the Protectorate.

Finishing my drink, I step back into the street. I can feel my anxiety begin to envelop me as I wonder how the mission will go. Will there only be one or two people on the ground, as I had expected? What if there are more? Will the element of surprise be enough?

All the way back home, I continue to ask myself such questions.
