# Salt

As I reach the police station the next morning, I receive a message.

“Officer Blackthorne, please report to meeting room seven.”

Making my way up the stairs, I find the room and enter.

“Claudius, glad you could make it,” says the police chief.

I sit down in one of the unoccupied chairs.

“So, last night, our technicians were able to recover some of the corrupted data from Kiln’s transmission.”

They will say her name. They will say her name. They will say her name.

I know what I must do. I have vowed to protect her life, and that of our unborn child, and I will do everything in my power to do so—even if this means forfeiting my own.

Feeling sick, I reach into my coat pocket.

My gun feels cold; much colder than usual.

I will never meet my child.

“How much of the data were they able to recover?” I ask.

“Some,” the chief replies.

“What was recovered?” someone asks.

“A physical trait of the person that was near Kiln at the time of his death.”

No. No. No. No.

“Which is?” I ask.

“It is quite common for people, right before dying, to notice or think about rather absurd things. In Kiln’s case, he was thinking about what looked like a birthmark on the hand of the person near him.”

*I love you,* I whisper.

An explosion happens outside, drawing everyone’s attention.

More bombings. People have become numb to them. They don’t even run anymore, akin to hearing a train horn, or a dog bark.

“As I was saying,” the chief continues, “he was focusing on this person’s birthmark. Additionally, he was thinking of death, or murder. This part is not certain.”

“So the murderer has a birthmark on their hand?” someone asks.

I feel like my chest is about to explode.

“Not quite,” the chief replies.

“What do you mean?” someone asks.

“The report from the coroner has also come in. Kiln died as a result of broken glass and shrapnel entering his body.”

“So he was not murdered?” I ask.

“He was killed by the enemy, but no, he was not murdered by one of the Rellies.”

After a few moments, the director continues, “It goes without saying that there is little point in continuing this investigation. However, we can not release this to the public. We still don’t know what Kiln was doing there.”

“So we will say that he died on a secret operation,” I say, my hand now outside of my jacket, resting on my leg. “He died a hero’s death, fighting the Shogunate, and that’s all that there is to know.”

The director looks at me and smiles.

“I like that. All in agreement?”

Everyone at the table nods.

“It’s settled then. Meeting adjourned.”

As I stand up, dabbing my sweaty forehead with the sleeve of my coat, the director addresses me, “Blackthorne, would you mind staying behind for a moment?”

I freeze.

“Yes, sir.”

As the room empties out, he approaches me, now standing only a couple of feet away.

Close. Far too close.

“What a terrible mess this is,” he says, looking around the now empty room.

“Yes, sir. It is a shame that Officer Kiln went out this way.”

“Yes, indeed,” he says, looking over my shoulder, absentmindedly.

“How’s Sarah?” he continues, his eyes darting to meet mine.

I can feel my heart begin to race.

With every fiber of my being, I try to keep my breathing steady.

“You know,” he continues, “I’m old enough to still remember a time before all of this, when people spoke to each other.”

“The data stream was not incomplete, was it?”

“It was partially incomplete, however, I did leave out a small detail during the meeting. The birthmark in question was shaped like a semicircle, forming a kind of smile.”

I can’t bring myself to say anything, and so I remain quiet.

“I hear you’ll be a father soon,” he adds.

The director places his hand on my shoulder.

*I’m happy for you.*

*You knew.*

The old man in front of me smiles, slides his hands in his pockets, and walks towards the door.

“Quite a common type of birthmark to have, don’t you think? Even if we tried, we’d likely never find the Rellie in question. I’ll see you back in on Monday, yes?”

My cheeks feel wet.

Why can I taste salt?

“Yes, sir.”
