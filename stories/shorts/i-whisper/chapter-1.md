A warm summer breeze fills my Police cruiser as I drive past a large advertising panel. Playing on a loop, the video displays a woman smiling as she zips her mouth closed. My lips barely moving, I mimic the message that follows.

*Honorable citizens only use neuralspeek.*

I slam my steering wheel in frustration. I can remember the days of my childhood, when neuralbond was new, and exciting. It promised to connect people, and make us safe.

Moving my hand up to my neck, I turn on the radio, data now being beamed into my mind via the neuralbond implant.

“Here is today’s news, for the 4th of July 2135. A damper has fallen on the celebrations today, as the war rages on. We’ll see more than one type of fireworks tonight, isn’t that right, Tom?”

“That’s right, Jean. And now, here is a report on the bombing that took place last night. The area around the Presidio of San Francisco, as well as the Golden Gate Bridge were destroyed, totaling fifty-four deaths. I guess they celebrated the 4th of July a bit too early, huh Jean?”

“Boy, I sure hope they had insurance,” she replies.

Reaching for my neck, I tap the implant, severing the connection. My eyes dart to the horizon, where until last night, the bridge overshadowed the landscape. The structure, now broken, slumps into the narrow strait.

I am now numb to the sound of falling bombs. Sometimes I worry they have become so familiar I might not notice the attacks all together.

The war with the Japanese Shogunate continues. Three years in, with no end in sight.

“I did it, OK? You’re right. I did it.”

My head whips around, trying to identify the source of the neuralspill. To my left, I see a distraught man, hunching over a crying woman. All around them, people struggling to pretend that they had not received the same data packet.

Poor bastard.

I’ve seen this more times than I can count. A husband cheats on his wife, and eventually, a neuralspill happens. His shame is broadcast to everyone in his vicinity. The people at the IT lab told me that neuralspill is caused by a phenomenon called neuralflood. Back in the day, we had relays everywhere. A mental communication would be converted into data packets, and sent over relays, so that they may reach their intended recipient.

When the war started, the Shogunate began targeting the relays, in order to hinder communication, and thus, the technology was expanded. If a data packet had to be transmitted, but a relay was not present in the vicinity, it would simply jump to the nearest person. From there, it would jump from individual to individual, until it made its way to a relay.

I asked the people at the lab why the technology needed relays in the first place. They said that every data packet had to pass through our servers, so that we may analyze their contents.

After the new patch was uploaded to everyone’s neuralbond implant, some people began to experience headaches. Soon after, they would find themselves spilling their thoughts through neuralspeek. Apparently this happens when too many data packets are routed through one individual, one node in the network, as they try to reach a relay.

Over to my right, I can see another large display.

*Only criminals use fleshspeek.*

When the technology was introduced, the authorities began to discourage using one’s voice. Neuralspeek was much more efficient, and convenient. Suddenly, verbal contracts became binding. If you communicated via neuralspeek, it would be recorded. In the event of a lawsuit, the transcripts would be pulled.

One’s word became their bond.

Over time, vocalizing became less common, and was eventually branded as fleshspeek.

In the end, it was made illegal.

I don’t like that name; fleshspeek. I prefer to think of it as whispering. You do it quietly, in order not to be heard. You do it with people to whom you are close, so that your relationship remains private, and thus only yours.

Turning left, I enter the parking lot of the San Francisco Police Department.

As I arrive at the entrance, I reach out my right hand, and press my finger against a flat metal surface. A small needle punctures my skin, extracting a drop of blood for immediate analysis. As the doors open, I look down at the display, now flashing green. Next to my photo, I can see my stats.

Claudius Blackthorne, 25, Officer

50% Irish, 25% English, 15% French, 10% Swedish

“Hey Claudius, wait up.”

Recognizing Thadeus’s neuralvoice, I turn around to greet him. I’ve never heard his actual voice.

I wonder what he sounds like.

Still on the other side of the barrier, Thadeus places his finger on the scanner, and a drop of blood is taken. I peer down at the screen, as it flashes green.

Thadeus, 23, Officer

50% Nigerian, 50% Angolan 

“I was looking for you. Have you heard the news?” Thadeus says.

“No, I just got in.”

“Kiln died last night. Killed by Rellies, apparently. We have the place on lock down, nobody goes in or out.”

“Kiln is dead?”

“Yeah, didn’t you—Important Message: Officer Kiln has been killed last night. Report to the station as soon as possible—get the memo?”

I stop for a moment, dazed. A few other people who had just come in share the experience, as we sync with the nearby relay.

“Did it just come through?” Thadeus asks.

“Yeah. Damn relays, they’re becoming few and far between. Delayed messages are becoming a real problem.”

“That’s above our pay grade. We need to head out. The chief says we need to report to the crime scene.”

“OK. Let’s go.”

We turn around, get into Thadeus’s cruiser, and head towards what is left of the Presidio of San Francisco.

“Did you know that initially there were loads of them?” Thadeus asks.

“Who?”

“The Rellies. They protested neuralbond technology. Said it was an abomination. Eventually, more and more of them chose to get the implant, and now they have neural-only sermons. Some claim a direct neural connection to God himself.”

“And Kiln was killed by one of these religious lunatics? Sorry, one of the Rellies?”

“Apparently so. What’s left of them hide out in an abandoned theater near the Golden Gate.”

“Where was Kiln found?”

“Right in the middle of their compound,” Thadeus replies.

“What? Why was he there?”

“I’m not sure. Nobody knows. Late last night we received a transmission from his implant, relayed through a secondary node. It was delayed by a few hours, but the original data packet was sent at the precise time of last night’s bombings. We think that the interference caused by the explosion corrupted the transmission, so only parts of it were deciphered.”

“What did it say?”

“Something about death, or killing. We didn’t hear from him after that, so an investigation was launched, and then the Rellies reported the body.”

“Do we know for sure one of them killed Kiln?”

“No, but he was one of our brothers. We’ll find out.”

As we near the Rellie theater, I can smell smoke. Even though the fires were put out hours ago, the odor still persists. Thadeus parks his cruiser next to the other police vehicles, and we step outside onto the hot tarmac.

Pushing the cordon aside, we make our way inside the building. As my eyes adjust to the dark, I can see small groups of Rellies sitting on the floor. Some are crying, while many look down at their shoes, resigned to their fate. Over to my left, I see a copy of the Hagakure.

Type D contraband. After the war with the Shogunate started, the Government became suspicious of Japanese culture, and its influence, so they banned it. This led to a secondary, unofficial law, which marked Japanese-American citizens as potential Shogunate loyalists, and thus relegated them to an underclass.

I shake my head, reminding myself that this is not why we are here.

“Poor bastards,” Thadeus says.

In front of us, an old yellowed poster—barely hanging onto the wall—says “Neuralbond is our future”.

Someone had crossed out *future*, replacing it with *crime*.

“How many of them are Japanese?” I ask.

“Most of them,” says Thadeus. “Many of them were born upstanding citizens, with neuralbond implants, but once the war started, many struggled to find employment. In the end, they fell in with the remaining Rellies.”

Sensing movement to my right, I turn my head. A small child holding a rag doll peers at us from behind a partly opened door. Even in the dimly lit room, I can see that her face is wet.

I choose to look away.

“Feeling betrayed by society, many refuse to engage in neuralspeek at all. Some have ripped out their implants,” continues Thadeus, as we near the crime scene.

“So they only use fleshspeek, making them criminals by default.”

“Pretty much,” says Thadeus.

We stop, having found the body. Kiln lay motionless on the hardwood floor, covered in cuts.

“What did this?” I ask one of the people working on the scene.

“It’s hard to tell,” someone answers.

I look around, but I’m not sure who’s speaking. I close my eyes in frustration, before I continue.

“Could they be knife cuts?”

“Yes, they could. However, we also found shards of glass embedded in his body. It is possible that he was stabbed, and then moved here, in front of the window, so that the shards from the explosion might cover the crime. We’re not sure yet.”

A door behind us opens. I turn around to see a woman in handcuffs, being escorted out by fellow officers.

“That’s Ichika Carrington,” Thadeus says. “The second in command.”

“And Eriksson?” I ask.

“The Prophet? He’s not here, but we’ll find him.”
